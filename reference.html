Topic 1: ITIL 4 Foundation Concepts

What Is ITIL 4?

ITIL (Information Technology Infrastructure Library) is a framework of best practices for IT service management (ITSM) and IT asset management (ITAM) that focuses on aligning IT services with business needs. ITIL 4, released in February 2019, is the current major version. It replaced ITIL v3/2011 with a modernized approach that addresses digital transformation, cloud computing, Agile, Lean, and DevOps integration.

ITIL 4 is owned by PeopleCert (since 2021, previously AXELOS). Certification is available only for individuals, not organizations — there is no formal "ITIL-compliant organization" certification.

Core philosophy shift: ITIL 4 moves from prescribing rigid processes to providing flexible practices and a holistic value-creation model. It answers "why" value is created rather than just "how" services are delivered.

How ITIL 4 Differs from ITIL v3

Aspect ITIL v3/2011 ITIL 4

Key structural changes from v3 to v4:

  • The 5-stage Service Lifecycle (Service Strategy, Design, Transition, Operation, Continual Improvement) was replaced by the Service Value System and its Service Value Chain.
  • "Processes" became "Practices" — a practice is broader than a process. It includes the processes, procedures, people, vendors, skills, data, and tools necessary to deliver a capability. A process is just one component within a practice.
  • Release and Deployment Management was split into two separate practices (Release Management + Deployment Management) to align with DevOps.
  • Configuration Management was split into IT Asset Management and Service Configuration Management.
  • Change Management was renamed Change Enablement to align with Agile methods.
  • ITIL 4 introduced the Customer Journey concept with 7 steps: Explore, Engage, Offer, Agree, Onboard, Co-create, Realize.

"Practice" vs "Process" in ITIL 4

Process (ITIL v3): A structured set of activities designed to accomplish a specific objective. Processes had defined inputs, outputs, roles, and responsibilities. They were the primary building block of ITIL v3.

Practice (ITIL 4): A set of organizational resources designed for performing work or accomplishing an objective. Practices are broader — they encompass: - Processes and procedures - People and roles - Technology and tools - Information and data - Partners and suppliers - Organizational culture

This shift acknowledges that delivering a capability requires more than just a well-defined process flow — it requires the entire ecosystem of resources around it.

The Service Value System (SVS)

The SVS is ITIL 4's overarching model showing how all organizational components work together to create value. It takes opportunity/demand as input and produces value as output.

The 5 components of the SVS:

  1. Guiding Principles — Recommendations that guide an organization in all circumstances, regardless of changes to its goals, strategies, type of work, or management structure. They are universal and enduring.

  2. Governance — The means by which an organization is directed and controlled. Governance evaluates, directs, and monitors all organizational activities — ensuring alignment with strategic objectives, managing risk, and providing accountability structures.

  3. Service Value Chain — The central element of the SVS. A flexible operating model with 6 activities for creating, delivering, and improving services. (Detailed below.)

  4. Practices — 34 sets of organizational resources designed for performing work. Organized into 3 categories. (Listed below.)

  5. Continual Improvement — A recurring organizational activity performed at all levels to ensure that performance meets stakeholder expectations. Involves questioning the status quo, identifying improvement opportunities, and implementing changes. Operates across the entire SVS, not just within the value chain.

The Service Value Chain — 6 Activities

The Service Value Chain is the flexible operating model at the center of the SVS. It is NOT a linear pipeline — activities interconnect and can be combined in different sequences to create value streams (specific workflows for particular scenarios).

# Activity Purpose
1 Plan Ensure a shared understanding of the vision, current status, and improvement direction for all 4 dimensions and all products/services. Produces strategic, tactical, and operational plans; portfolio decisions; architecture requirements; policies.
2 Improve Ensure continual improvement of products, services, and practices across all value chain activities and the 4 dimensions. Produces improvement initiatives, status reports, updated continual improvement register.
3 Engage Provide a good understanding of stakeholder needs, maintain transparency, and foster good relationships. Handles all interactions with customers, users, partners, and colleagues. Produces demand/opportunity insights, requirements, SLAs, experience-level agreements (XLAs), service reports.
4 Design & Transition Ensure that products and services continually meet stakeholder expectations for quality, cost, and time-to-market. Covers design of new/changed services and their transition into live operation. Produces service specifications, new/changed services, performance data.
5 Obtain/Build Ensure that service components are available when and where they are needed, and meet agreed specifications. Covers procurement, development, and integration of service components. Produces contracts, completed components, performance data.
6 Deliver & Support Ensure that services are delivered and supported according to agreed specifications and stakeholder expectations. The operational activity. Produces delivered services, support documentation, change requests, improvement opportunities.

How they interconnect (example — incident resolution value stream): 1. Engage: User reports an issue (incident created) 2. Deliver & Support: Service desk triages and attempts resolution 3. Design & Transition: If a code fix is needed, development team designs a fix 4. Obtain/Build: Fix is built/tested 5. Deliver & Support: Fix is deployed, service restored 6. Engage: User confirms resolution 7. Improve: Feedback collected, improvement register updated 8. Plan: Future improvements planned based on lessons learned

The 4 Dimensions of Service Management

Every service, practice, and value chain activity must be considered across all 4 dimensions to be effective. Neglecting any dimension creates imbalance.

  1. Organizations and People
  2. Organizational structure, culture, and governance
  3. Roles, responsibilities, and authorities
  4. Staff capacity, competency, and skills
  5. Leadership and communication

  6. How culture supports strategic objectives

  7. Information and Technology

  8. Information and knowledge needed for service management
  9. Technologies required (applications, databases, communication tools, AI, cloud, etc.)
  10. Information architecture and data management

  11. Relationships between information components

  12. Partners and Suppliers

  13. External organizations involved in design, deployment, delivery, support, and improvement of services
  14. Contracts and agreements
  15. Levels of integration (from separate/commodity to fully integrated/partnership)

  16. Supplier management strategies (service integration models)

  17. Value Streams and Processes

  18. Value streams: End-to-end sequences of activities that create value for stakeholders
  19. Processes: Structured sets of activities that transform inputs into outputs
  20. How different parts of the organization work together in an integrated, coordinated way
  21. Workflow design and optimization

External factor: PESTLE (Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Legal, Environmental) factors constrain how an organization works across all 4 dimensions.

The 7 Guiding Principles of ITIL 4

These are universal recommendations applicable in all circumstances. They guide decision-making and behavior at every level.

  1. Focus on Value
  2. Everything the organization does should map back to value for stakeholders (customers, users, sponsor, and the organization itself)
  3. Understand who the service consumer is and what they value

  4. Value is not always financial — positive employee and customer experience are also important measures

  5. Start Where You Are

  6. Do not start from scratch without first assessing the current state
  7. Understand what already exists and can be leveraged
  8. Measure and observe directly rather than relying on assumptions

  9. Risk: building something new that duplicates or conflicts with what already works

  10. Progress Iteratively with Feedback

  11. Do not attempt to do everything at once
  12. Organize work into smaller, manageable sections (iterations) that can be executed and completed in a timely manner
  13. Use feedback before, during, and after each iteration to focus efforts

  14. Faster feedback loops enable faster course correction

  15. Collaborate and Promote Visibility

  16. Work across boundaries — collaboration among teams, stakeholders, partners
  17. Make work visible (no hidden agendas, no shadow IT decisions)
  18. Shared understanding reduces rework, improves communication, and builds trust

  19. Insufficient visibility leads to poor decision-making

  20. Think and Work Holistically

  21. No service, practice, process, department, or supplier operates in isolation
  22. Outcomes suffer when work is done in silos
  23. Understand the bigger picture — how all parts connect to create value

  24. Consider all 4 dimensions for every service and practice

  25. Keep It Simple and Practical

  26. Always use the minimum number of steps needed to accomplish an objective
  27. Eliminate activities that produce no value (waste)
  28. If a process, service, action, or metric provides no value, eliminate it

  29. Outcome-based thinking, not process-for-process-sake

  30. Optimize and Automate

  31. Optimize first (make it effective), then automate (make it efficient)
  32. Never automate a broken or wasteful process — that just makes waste faster
  33. Use technology and automation to maximize human and organizational efficiency
  34. Standardize and streamline before investing in automation tools

Key ITIL 4 Terminology

Service: A means of enabling value co-creation by facilitating outcomes that customers want to achieve, without the customer having to manage specific costs and risks.

Service Management: A set of specialized organizational capabilities for enabling value for customers in the form of services.

Value: The perceived benefits, usefulness, and importance of something. Value is subjective — determined by the consumer's perception, not the provider's intent.

Value Co-creation: Value is not delivered by a provider to a consumer — it is co-created through an active collaboration between provider and consumer (and other stakeholders). Both parties contribute.

Outcome: A result for a stakeholder enabled by one or more outputs. What the customer actually wants to achieve. Example: A photo album (output) enables the preservation of memories (outcome).

Output: A tangible or intangible deliverable of an activity. Example: A printed report, a configured server, a deployed application.

Cost: The amount of money spent on a specific activity or resource. From the consumer's perspective, there are two types: - Costs removed: Costs the consumer no longer needs to bear because the service handles them (part of the value proposition). Example: No need to hire and train specialized staff. - Costs imposed: Costs the consumer must bear to consume the service. Example: Subscription fees, training costs, procurement of network access.

Risk: A possible event that could cause harm or loss, or make it more difficult to achieve objectives. Uncertainty of outcome. Two types from the consumer perspective: - Risks removed: Risks the consumer no longer needs to manage because the service handles them. Example: Service provider manages hardware failure risk. - Risks imposed: New risks introduced by consuming the service. Example: Dependency on the service provider, data security in a shared environment.

Utility: The functionality offered by a product or service to meet a particular need. Determines whether a service is "fit for purpose" — does it do what it's supposed to do? A service must either support the consumer's performance or remove constraints from the consumer.

Warranty: The assurance that a product or service will meet agreed requirements. Determines whether a service is "fit for use" — does it perform well enough? Warranty addresses availability, capacity, continuity, and security. Both utility AND warranty are required for a service to deliver value.

Stakeholder roles: - Customer: Defines service requirements and takes responsibility for outcomes of service consumption - User: Uses the service (may be different from the customer) - Sponsor: Authorizes budget for service consumption

Service Offering: A formal description of one or more services designed to address the needs of a target consumer group. May include: goods (supplied to consumer), access to resources (permitted under agreed terms), and service actions (performed to address a consumer's needs).

Service Relationship: A cooperation between a service provider and service consumer, including service provision, service consumption, and service relationship management.

Topic 2: Incident Management

Definition

Incident: An unplanned interruption to an IT service or reduction in the quality of an IT service. This includes both actual service failures and potential failures (e.g., a single disk failure in a RAID array — the service is still running, but its quality/resilience is reduced).

The purpose of the Incident Management practice is to minimize the negative impact of incidents by restoring normal service operation as quickly as possible.

Key distinction: Incident Management focuses solely on restoring service (fighting symptoms). It does NOT investigate root causes — that is Problem Management's job.

Incident Lifecycle

1. Detection and Notification

Incidents are identified through: - User/customer reports — calls, emails, self-service portal submissions - Automated monitoring and event management — alerts from monitoring tools - Technical staff observation — staff notice degraded performance or errors - Supplier/vendor notifications — third-party service providers report issues

2. Logging

Every incident MUST be logged as an Incident Record, regardless of how it was reported. The record should capture: - Unique identifier - Date/time of detection and logging - Reporting source/channel - Contact details of the reporter - Description of symptoms - Affected service(s) and configuration item(s) - Category - Priority - Status (open, in progress, resolved, closed) - Complete history from registration through closure

3. Categorization

Classification of the incident by type/area to: - Route to the correct support group - Enable trend analysis and reporting - Match against known errors and existing problems - Determine available resolution timeframes

Categories are typically multi-level (e.g., Hardware > Server > Disk Failure) and should align with the service catalog.

4. Prioritization

Priority is determined by a matrix of Impact and Urgency:

Impact — The effect on the business: - High: Large number of staff/customers affected, financial impact exceeding threshold (e.g., >$10,000), reputational damage, risk of injury - Medium: Moderate number of users affected, moderate financial impact (e.g., $1,000-$10,000) - Low: Minimal users affected, negligible financial impact (e.g., <$1,000)

Urgency — How quickly the business needs resolution: - High: Damage increases rapidly over time, time-sensitive work blocked, VIP users affected - Medium: Damage increases considerably over time - Low: Damage increases minimally over time, work is not time-sensitive

Priority Matrix

Impact \ Urgency High Medium Low
High P1 — Critical P2 — High P3 — Medium
Medium P2 — High P3 — Medium P4 — Low
Low P3 — Medium P4 — Low P5 — Very Low

Target Response and Resolution Times (typical)

Priority Level Target Response Target Resolution
P1 Critical Immediate 1 hour
P2 High 10 minutes 4 hours
P3 Medium 1 hour 8 hours
P4 Low 4 hours 24 hours
P5 Very Low 1 business day 1 week

Note: These are illustrative targets. Each organization defines its own SLA-based targets.

5. Initial Diagnosis (1st Level Support)

  • Service desk attempts immediate resolution using:
  • Knowledge base articles
  • Known Error Database (KEDB) entries
  • Pre-defined Incident Models (standardized step-by-step procedures for common incident types)
  • Self-help information and automated resolution tools
  • Match against existing incidents, problems, and known errors
  • If resolved: proceed to closure
  • If not resolved: escalate (functional escalation)

6. Escalation

Functional Escalation (horizontal): - Transfer from 1st-level to 2nd-level support (specialist groups within IT) - Transfer from 2nd-level to 3rd-level support (vendor/manufacturer expertise) - Based on skill/capability — the current group lacks the expertise to resolve

Hierarchical Escalation (vertical): - Escalation up the management chain (team lead, manager, director, CIO) - Triggered by time breaches — resolution time approaching SLA limits - Triggered by severity — high-impact incidents requiring management visibility - Ensures resources and authority are applied to critical incidents

Both types can happen simultaneously. A P1 incident might be functionally escalated to a specialist team while simultaneously hierarchically escalated to the IT director.

7. Investigation and Diagnosis (2nd/3rd Level Support)

  • In-depth technical investigation
  • May involve vendor/manufacturer engagement (3rd-level)
  • If no resolution found, a Problem Record is created and handed to Problem Management
  • If a workaround is found, it may be applied while a permanent fix is developed

8. Resolution and Recovery

  • Implement the fix or workaround
  • Verify service is restored to normal operation
  • Confirm with the user/customer that the issue is resolved

9. Closure

  • 1st-level support (Service Desk) performs final quality control:
  • Verify the incident is actually resolved
  • Confirm user satisfaction
  • Ensure the incident record is complete and accurate
  • Classify the incident for reporting/trend analysis
  • Determine if the incident could recur (if so, raise a Problem record)
  • Close the incident record

Incident Models

An Incident Model is a pre-defined set of steps for handling a particular type of incident. Used for routine, recurring incident types to ensure consistent, efficient handling. Examples: password reset failures, printer connectivity issues, VPN connection drops.

Each model defines: - Steps to take and their order - Responsibilities at each step - Timescales and escalation thresholds - Required evidence/documentation

Major Incident Management

A major incident causes serious interruption of business activities and must be resolved with greater urgency than standard incidents. It is NOT defined by technical complexity — it is defined by business impact.

What makes an incident "major": - Affects mission-critical systems or large user populations - Causes significant financial loss, reputational damage, or regulatory risk - Requires immediate cross-functional collaboration - Cannot be handled through standard incident processes alone

Common triggers: - Core business application outage - Data center or infrastructure failure - Cybersecurity breach or data loss - System-wide performance degradation during peak hours - Integration failures between critical systems

Major Incident Process (4 stages):

  1. Identification and Logging
  2. Clear pre-defined criteria determine what qualifies as "major"
  3. Immediate logging with timestamps, affected services, impact summary

  4. Modern ITSM tools can auto-classify using AI or pre-defined rules

  5. Investigation and Diagnosis

  6. Major Incident Manager takes control
  7. "War room" / bridge call assembled with cross-functional specialists
  8. Controlled urgency: gather data, assign roles, track hypotheses

  9. Continuous stakeholder communication ("Even 'we're still investigating' reassures stakeholders")

  10. Resolution and Recovery

  11. Priority is restoration over perfection — temporary fixes are acceptable
  12. Change management processes may be expedited (but still documented)

  13. All modifications documented for post-incident review

  14. Post-Incident Review (PIR)

  15. Conducted after service restoration (typically within 48-72 hours)
  16. Blameless, data-driven analysis
  17. Covers: root cause, detection effectiveness, escalation speed, communication quality
  18. Feeds into Problem Management for permanent fixes
  19. Updates playbooks, documentation, and training

Major Incident Roles: - Major Incident Manager: Central coordinator; prioritizes actions, controls communication, documents decisions - Service Desk Analysts: First responders who detect and escalate - Technical Support Teams: Engineers who investigate and resolve - Communications Lead: Manages internal and external messaging - Problem Manager: Engages post-crisis for root cause analysis - Executive Sponsor: Approves extraordinary actions in critical scenarios

Communication Protocol: - Designate a single communication channel (Slack, Teams) - Use timestamped, documented decisions - Assign a dedicated communications lead - Use pre-written message templates for consistency - Define regular update intervals - Maintain a RACI matrix clarifying ownership during major incidents

Incident vs Problem — The Key Distinction

Aspect Incident Problem
Definition Unplanned interruption or quality reduction Root cause of one or more incidents
Focus Restore service ASAP Find and eliminate root cause
Timeframe Short-term (minutes to hours) Long-term (days to weeks)
Approach Reactive — fight the symptoms Both reactive and proactive
Resolution Workaround or fix to restore service Permanent elimination of the cause
Relationship One incident may have one underlying problem One problem may cause many incidents

Incident Management KPIs

Speed Metrics: - MTTA (Mean Time to Acknowledge): Average time between incident detection/report and first acknowledgment. Formula: Sum of (acknowledgment time - alert time) / number of incidents - FRT (First Response Time): Time between ticket submission and first agent reply. Formula: First agent response - ticket creation - MTTR (Mean Time to Resolve): Average time from incident report to full resolution. Formula: Total resolution time / number of incidents. Benchmark: Many teams aim for under 4 business hours for standard incidents.

Quality Metrics: - FCR (First Contact Resolution): Percentage of incidents resolved on first contact without escalation or reopening. Formula: (Tickets resolved on first contact / total tickets) x 100 - CSAT (Customer Satisfaction): User satisfaction measured through post-closure surveys. Formula: (Positive survey responses / total responses) x 100

Control and Reliability Metrics: - SLA Compliance: Percentage of incidents resolved within agreed SLA timeframes. Formula: (Tickets resolved within SLA / total applicable tickets) x 100 - Escalation Rate: Percentage of incidents requiring escalation. Formula: (Escalated incidents / total incidents) x 100 - Reopen Rate: Percentage of closed incidents that are reopened. Formula: (Reopened incidents / closed incidents) x 100. High reopen rates indicate inadequate resolution quality. - Incident Backlog: Count of unresolved incidents at period end. Growing backlogs indicate capacity or process issues. - Incident Volume by Priority: Count of incidents broken down by priority level (P1-P5). Trending analysis reveals systemic issues.

Major Incident Specific Metrics: - MIF (Major Incident Frequency): Number of major incidents per time period — indicator of system health - MTBMI (Mean Time Between Major Incidents): Average time between major incidents — measures organizational resilience - TTC (Time to Communicate): How quickly stakeholders are informed after escalation - PIR Completion Rate: Percentage of major incidents that complete post-incident review — measures learning culture

Topic 3: Service Request Management

Definition

Service Request: A request from a user or user's authorized representative that initiates an agreed service action that is a normal part of service delivery. Service requests are NOT incidents — they do not involve a service failure or degradation.

The purpose of the Service Request Management practice is to support the agreed quality of a service by handling all pre-defined, user-initiated service requests in an effective and user-friendly manner.

How Service Requests Differ from Incidents

Aspect Service Request Incident
Nature Planned, expected, routine Unplanned, unexpected
Cause User wants something Something is broken or degraded
Urgency Generally low; follows standard SLAs Can be critical; based on impact/urgency
Process Pre-defined fulfillment workflow Diagnosis and troubleshooting
Typical SLA 3-5 business days (standard) 1-24 hours (depending on priority)
Example "I need access to SharePoint" "SharePoint is down for everyone"
Risk Low — standard, pre-approved actions Variable — depends on what broke

Types of Service Requests

  1. Service delivery actions — Routine actions: generating reports, replacing toner, performing scheduled maintenance
  2. Information requests — "How do I do X?", "What is the status of Y?"
  3. Resource/service provisioning — Providing laptops, phones, virtual machines, accounts, software installations
  4. Access requests — Granting/revoking permissions to folders, applications, systems
  5. Feedback and complaints — Comments about service quality, usability suggestions

Common Examples of IT Service Requests

  • Password resets
  • New user account creation / onboarding
  • Software installation requests
  • Hardware provisioning (laptop, monitor, phone)
  • VPN access setup
  • Email distribution list changes
  • Network printer setup
  • File/folder permission changes
  • Conference room AV equipment setup
  • New virtual machine provisioning
  • Application access requests
  • Mobile device enrollment (MDM)
  • Standard change requests (pre-approved changes)

Service Catalog — What It Is and Why It Matters

Definition: A single source of consistent information on all services and service offerings, available to the relevant audience.

Purpose: "To provide a single source of consistent information on all services and service offerings, and to ensure that it is available to the relevant audience."

The service catalog is the front door of Service Request Management. It is where users go to see what services are available, what they can request, and how to request it.

Why it matters: - User empowerment: Users can browse and select services without guessing or calling the help desk - Standardization: Every request follows a consistent, documented workflow - Automation enablement: Catalog entries can be linked to automated fulfillment workflows - Expectation management: Clear SLAs and timelines per service - Demand management: IT can see what users are requesting and plan capacity accordingly - Reporting and optimization: Catalog usage data reveals trends, popular services, and underutilized offerings

A good catalog entry includes: - Service name and description - Who can request it (eligibility) - What information is needed from the requester - Approval requirements (if any) - Expected fulfillment timeline - Cost (if applicable — chargeback/showback) - Fulfillment group responsible

Request Fulfillment Workflow

  1. Initiation: User submits a request via self-service portal, service catalog, email, phone, or chat
  2. Logging and Categorization: Request is recorded, categorized by type, and validated (is the requester authorized?)
  3. Authorization/Approval: Approval requirements depend on the request type:
  4. Standard/pre-approved requests: Minimal or no approval (e.g., password reset)
  5. Pre-approved with manager sign-off: Manager approval via automated workflow (e.g., software license)
  6. Complex/non-standard requests: May require Change Advisory Board (CAB) review
  7. Fulfillment (Request Model Execution): Appropriate fulfillment group executes the request following the documented Request Model (standard steps for that request type)
  8. Monitoring and Escalation: Service desk monitors progress, escalates if SLA breach is approaching
  9. Closure and Evaluation: Quality control, requester notification, satisfaction feedback, documentation for improvement

Standard vs Non-Standard Requests

Standard Requests: - Pre-defined, well-documented, frequently occurring - Follow a fixed Request Model with specific steps - Low risk, pre-approved or require minimal approval - Often automated or semi-automated - Examples: password reset, standard software install, new user onboarding

Non-Standard / Customized Requests: - Unusual, complex, or first-time requests - No existing Request Model — may need ad-hoc workflow - Higher risk — may require assessment, CAB review - May trigger Change Enablement if infrastructure changes are involved - Examples: custom application deployment, non-standard hardware configuration, one-off data migration

Self-Service Portals and Their Role

A self-service portal is a user-facing web interface that allows users to: - Browse the service catalog and submit requests without contacting the help desk - Track request status in real-time without calling for updates - Access knowledge base articles for self-resolution - View announcements about planned outages or known issues

Why self-service matters: - Reduces call volume to the service desk (industry benchmarks: 20-40% reduction) - Empowers users — available 24/7, not limited to service desk hours - Standardizes request submission — structured forms capture required information - Enables automation — requests can trigger automated fulfillment workflows - Reduces fulfillment time — no queuing for service desk agent availability - Provides data — usage patterns inform service improvement decisions

Success factors: - User-friendly design (if it's harder than emailing, users won't adopt it) - Mobile-friendly access - Intuitive categorization (users shouldn't need to know ITIL categories) - Search functionality - Status visibility and notifications - Integration with approval workflows

Service Request Management KPIs

  • Average fulfillment time by category
  • SLA compliance rate (% fulfilled within agreed timeframes)
  • First-time fulfillment rate (no rejections or revisions needed)
  • Customer satisfaction (CSAT) scores
  • Self-service adoption rate (self-service vs. assisted request ratio)
  • Cost per request fulfilled
  • Request backlog (open request trend over time)
  • Request-to-incident conversion rate (requests that reveal underlying incidents)

Topic 4: Problem Management

Definition

Problem: The underlying cause of one or more incidents. The cause is typically unknown at the time a problem record is created.

Known Error: A problem that has a documented root cause and a workaround (but not necessarily a permanent fix).

The purpose of the Problem Management practice is to reduce the likelihood and impact of incidents by identifying actual and potential causes of incidents, and managing workarounds and known errors.

How Problems Relate to Incidents

The relationship is hierarchical: - An incident is a symptom — something broke or degraded - A problem is the disease — the underlying cause of why it broke - Multiple incidents may share a single underlying problem - Resolving the problem eliminates the recurring incidents

Example: - Incident 1: "Email is slow for Finance department" (Monday) - Incident 2: "Email is slow for Finance department" (Wednesday) - Incident 3: "Email crashes for Finance department" (Friday) - Problem: "Mail server has a memory leak in version 12.3 that triggers under high mailbox count"

The Incident-Problem-Change Triangle

These three practices form a tightly integrated cycle:

  1. Incidents trigger Problem investigation (reactive problem management)
  2. Problems are diagnosed to find root causes and become Known Errors
  3. Known Errors may require Changes to implement permanent fixes
  4. Changes resolve the underlying Problem
  5. Resolving the Problem prevents future Incidents
Incidents --> Problem Management --> Known Errors --> Change Enablement

Reactive vs Proactive Problem Management

Reactive Problem Management

  • Trigger: Major or recurring incidents that have already occurred
  • Approach: React to incidents, investigate their root cause after the fact
  • Activities:
  • Identifying problems from recurring incident patterns
  • Diagnosing root causes of known incidents
  • Creating workarounds for immediate relief
  • Submitting RFCs (Requests for Change) for permanent fixes
  • When to use: Starting point for organizations new to problem management; leverages existing service desk staff during non-incident periods

Proactive Problem Management

  • Trigger: Trend analysis and monitoring — BEFORE incidents happen
  • Approach: Actively hunt for potential problems using data analysis
  • Activities:
  • Analyzing incident records, operational logs, and monitoring data to find patterns and trends
  • Reviewing KEDB for known errors that might cause future incidents
  • Performing infrastructure health assessments
  • Analyzing configuration and change data for risk patterns
  • Engaging in major problem reviews for systemic improvement
  • When to use: As organizational maturity grows; requires analytically skilled staff with deep infrastructure knowledge
  • Challenge: Difficult to quantify ROI — you're solving problems that haven't happened yet

Maturity path: Start reactive, build toward proactive as capability and data grow.

Problem Lifecycle

  1. Problem Identification
  2. Reactive: Triggered by recurring incidents, major incidents, or incident patterns

  3. Proactive: Triggered by trend analysis, monitoring data, infrastructure reviews

  4. Problem Logging

  5. Record all relevant details: symptoms, affected services, related incidents, timeline

  6. Link to related incident records

  7. Problem Categorization and Prioritization

  8. Categorize using same scheme as incidents (for correlation)

  9. Prioritize based on: frequency of related incidents, impact on business, availability of workarounds, cost of investigation vs. cost of incidents continuing

  10. Problem Diagnosis (Problem Control)

  11. Root cause analysis using structured techniques (see below)
  12. Develop and test hypotheses
  13. Identify the root cause

  14. When root cause is identified: problem becomes a Known Error

  15. Known Error Recording

  16. Document the root cause
  17. Document any workaround
  18. Add to the Known Error Database (KEDB)

  19. Make available to Incident Management for faster future resolution

  20. Error Control

  21. Assess whether a permanent fix is feasible and cost-justified
  22. If yes: submit a Request for Change (RFC) to Change Enablement
  23. If no (too expensive, too risky, or impact is manageable): maintain the workaround
  24. Monitor effectiveness of workarounds

  25. Re-assess periodically as conditions change

  26. Problem Resolution and Closure

  27. After successful change implementation: verify the problem is resolved
  28. Close the problem record
  29. Update related incident records and KEDB entries

  30. Document lessons learned

  31. Major Problem Review

  32. Conducted for significant problems (similar to major incident PIR)
  33. Verify the problem is truly eliminated
  34. Extract lessons for process improvement
  35. Feed findings into continual improvement

Known Errors and the Known Error Database (KEDB)

Known Error: A problem where the root cause is documented and a workaround exists. It remains a "known error" until a permanent fix is implemented.

Known Error Database (KEDB): A centralized repository of all known error records. Maintained by Problem Management, accessed by both Problem Management and Incident Management.

KEDB contents per entry: - Known error identifier - Problem reference - Root cause description - Workaround description and instructions - Affected services and configuration items - Status (active, fix in progress, fix deployed, closed) - Related incidents - Date identified, last updated - Permanent fix plan (if any)

Why KEDB matters: - Incident Management can search the KEDB to find known workarounds, dramatically reducing resolution time - Enables first-level support to resolve incidents that would otherwise require escalation - Provides a clear record of what is broken and what is being done about it - Supports decision-making about where to invest in permanent fixes

Root Cause Analysis Techniques

ITIL 4 specifically recommends these techniques (per AXELOS Problem Management Practice Guide, 2020):

1. The 5 Whys

  • Ask "Why?" five successive times to drill progressively deeper toward the root cause
  • Best for: Simple to moderately complex problems
  • Limitation: Tends to follow a single line of inquiry, potentially missing multiple contributing causes
  • Example:
  • Why did the server crash? → Memory was exhausted
  • Why was memory exhausted? → A process was consuming 95% of RAM
  • Why was the process consuming 95%? → A memory leak in version 3.2
  • Why is version 3.2 still in production? → The upgrade was deprioritized
  • Why was it deprioritized? → No one tracked the vendor's advisory → Root cause: No process for tracking vendor security/stability advisories

2. Kepner-Tregoe (Kepner and Fourie Critical Thinking)

  • Structured problem analysis using a formal question template
  • Four key steps:
  • State and agree on the problem definition
  • Gather information (ask the right questions of the right people)
  • Organize and analyze information
  • Draw conclusions
  • Best for: Complex problems requiring systematic, disciplined analysis
  • Strength: Prevents premature solution evaluation by enforcing thorough problem understanding first

3. Fault Tree Analysis

  • Deductive, top-down visual technique
  • The undesired event (problem) sits at the top of the diagram
  • Investigation branches downward through all possible contributing factors
  • Uses Boolean logic gates (AND/OR) to show how combinations of failures lead to the top-level event
  • Best for: Complex systems with multiple potential failure points
  • Strength: Enables probabilistic risk assessment when numerical data is available

4. Ishikawa (Fishbone / Cause-and-Effect) Diagram

  • Visual "fishbone" structure organizing potential causes into categories
  • Standard categories (the "6 Ms"): Methods, Machines, Manpower, Materials, Measurement, Mother Nature (Environment)
  • Team brainstorming exercise: participants identify potential causes on sticky notes, then organize into fishbone branches
  • Best for: Exploring multiple potential causes across different domains
  • Strength: Encourages looking beyond the obvious and considering systemic factors

5. Chronological Analysis

  • Reconstruct the timeline of events leading to the problem
  • Map out what happened, when, in what order
  • Best for: Incidents where the sequence of events matters (cascading failures, change-related issues)

6. Pareto Analysis (80/20 Rule)

  • Analyze incident data to identify which problems cause the most incidents
  • Prioritize problem investigation based on frequency and impact
  • Best for: Deciding which problems to investigate first when resources are limited

Topic 5: Change Enablement (formerly Change Management)

Definition and Purpose

Change: The addition, modification, or removal of anything that could have a direct or indirect effect on services. This includes changes to IT infrastructure, applications, documentation, processes, supplier relationships, and any other element of IT service delivery.

Change Enablement is the ITIL 4 practice of ensuring that changes are implemented in a way that minimizes risk and disruption while maximizing value. It was renamed from "Change Management" (ITIL v3) to emphasize the shift from controlling and restricting change to enabling change to happen quickly and safely.

The analogy: Change Enablement is like brakes on a car — you do not use them all the time, but they are critical when needed. The goal is not to prevent motion but to make motion safe.

Key Shift from ITIL v3 to ITIL 4

Aspect ITIL v3 (Change Management) ITIL 4 (Change Enablement)
Philosophy Control and restrict change Enable and facilitate change
Authorization Centralized CAB for most changes Decentralized change authorities
Approval speed Weekly/scheduled CAB meetings Continuous/daily, often automated
Building block Standalone process in Service Transition Practice embedded across entire SVS
Integration Limited DevOps awareness Native CI/CD, Agile, DevOps integration
Change size Larger, consolidated releases Smaller, modular, frequent changes
Focus Process rigidity and documentation Practice integration and value delivery

ITIL 4 repositioned change from a single lifecycle stage (Service Transition) into a management practice embedded across the entire Service Value System. This means change governance applies at every stage of service delivery, not just during transitions.

Three Types of Changes

1. Standard Changes

Definition: Pre-approved, low-risk, repeatable modifications that follow a well-defined, documented procedure. They are pre-authorized because their risk profile is well understood and acceptable.

Characteristics: - Low risk, well understood - Follow established change models (documented step-by-step procedures) - Pre-approved — no additional per-instance authorization needed - Often automated or semi-automated - Documented in runbooks

Examples: - Password resets - Routine OS patches and software updates - Adding users to standard groups - Deploying pre-approved software from the catalog - Replacing toner/hardware consumables - Standard firewall rule additions for known application patterns - Scheduled database maintenance scripts

How they become "standard": A change type earns standard status by going through normal change assessment first, demonstrating low risk across multiple implementations, and then being formally pre-approved. The change model is documented and the change authority grants blanket pre-authorization for future instances.

2. Normal Changes

Definition: Changes that require individual risk assessment and formal approval before implementation. They are planned and follow the full change enablement workflow.

Characteristics: - Moderate to high risk depending on scope - Require a formal Request for Change (RFC) - Subject to risk and impact assessment - Require explicit authorization from the appropriate change authority - Scheduled for implementation during agreed maintenance windows

Sub-categories by risk level:

Sub-category Risk Level Approval
Minor normal Low-to-medium risk, limited impact Change Manager or team lead may approve
Significant normal Medium risk, broader impact Change Manager approval after assessment
Major normal High risk, enterprise-wide impact Full CAB review + senior management approval

Examples: - Server infrastructure upgrades - Database schema changes - Network topology modifications - Application version upgrades - New service deployments - Major security policy changes affecting all users

3. Emergency Changes

Definition: Changes that must be implemented as soon as possible to resolve a major incident, prevent an imminent critical failure, or apply an urgent security patch. They bypass the normal scheduling process but still require authorization.

Characteristics: - Time-critical — cannot wait for scheduled CAB meetings - Authorized by the Emergency Change Advisory Board (ECAB) - Still require documentation (often completed retrospectively) - MUST include a rollback plan - ALWAYS require a post-implementation review (PIR)

Examples: - Critical security patches for actively exploited vulnerabilities - Emergency fixes during major service outages - Server restoration after hardware failure - Firewall rule changes to block an active attack - Emergency database recovery procedures

Important: Emergency changes are NOT a shortcut for poor planning. Organizations that see a high percentage of emergency changes have a process problem — most changes should be plannable through normal channels.

Change Authority — Who Approves What

A change authority is a person or group responsible for authorizing changes. ITIL 4 decentralizes this concept — different changes can have different change authorities depending on risk and scope.

Change Type Change Authority Rationale
Standard Pre-approved (no per-instance authority needed) Risk is well understood and pre-accepted
Minor normal Team lead or change manager Low risk, limited scope
Significant normal Change manager (with CAB advice) Moderate risk, needs broader assessment
Major normal Senior management / executive board (with CAB advice) High risk, enterprise impact
Emergency ECAB (subset of CAB available on short notice) Urgent, cannot wait for scheduled review

In DevOps/CI-CD environments: Daily stand-ups replace formal approvals. Team leads serve as change authorities. User stories and acceptance criteria provide documentation. This enables near-continuous deployment (multiple times daily).

In Agile environments: Sprint reviews authorize changes pre-deployment. Developers assess risk directly. This avoids delays from traditional CAB processes.

The Change Advisory Board (CAB)

What It Is

The CAB is a group of people who advise the Change Manager on the assessment, prioritization, and scheduling of changes. It is a Change ADVISORY Board — it advises, it does not approve. The Change Manager retains final approval authority based on CAB recommendations.

CAB Membership (typical)

  • Change Manager (chairs the meeting)
  • Service Desk Manager — protects end-user experience
  • Operations Manager — assesses infrastructure impact
  • Application Engineer/Architect — reviews software modifications
  • Information Security Officer — identifies vulnerability and compliance risks
  • Senior Network Engineer — evaluates connectivity and network changes
  • Business Relationship Manager — ensures alignment with organizational strategy
  • Optional: Product owners, cloud architects, DevOps engineers, data protection officers

Membership may rotate based on the changes being reviewed. Not every member needs to attend every meeting — only those relevant to the changes on the agenda.

When CAB IS Needed

  • Normal changes with significant or major risk — changes affecting multiple services, large user populations, or critical infrastructure
  • Changes with cross-team dependencies — where multiple teams must coordinate
  • Changes impacting compliance or regulatory requirements
  • First-of-a-kind changes — no precedent, risk profile unknown
  • Changes requiring business stakeholder input — operational timing, business impact

When CAB is NOT Needed

  • Standard changes — pre-approved, low-risk, well-understood
  • Minor normal changes — where team leads can assess and authorize
  • Automated CI/CD deployments — where automated testing and peer review provide sufficient governance
  • Routine maintenance — OS patches, scheduled restarts, standard hardware replacements
  • Changes already authorized through other governance — sprint reviews, architectural review boards

CAB Meeting Cadence

  • Standard CAB: Weekly or bi-weekly (for planned normal changes)
  • ECAB: On-demand, available on short notice for emergencies

CAB Process Flow

  1. RFC Submission — Team submits Request for Change with complete documentation
  2. Initial Review — Change Manager verifies completeness, assigns priority, determines if CAB review is needed
  3. Risk and Impact Assessment — CAB evaluates technical, business, and security factors
  4. Recommendation — CAB advises approve, reject, or defer (with conditions)
  5. Authorization — Change Manager makes final decision based on CAB advice
  6. Scheduling — Approved changes are placed on the change schedule
  7. Post-Implementation Review — CAB validates outcomes after deployment

The ECAB (Emergency Change Advisory Board)

A subset of core CAB members available on short notice to handle emergency changes. The ECAB: - Operates with a smaller, faster-decision group - Available outside regular business hours - Reviews and authorizes emergency changes in real time (phone call, Teams/Slack, etc.) - Requires retrospective documentation and a mandatory PIR

Common Problems with CABs

Problem Solution
CAB becomes a bottleneck — all changes queue for weekly meeting Delegate authority for low-risk changes; only route significant changes to CAB
"Rubber stamp" culture — CAB approves everything without real review Focus CAB on high-risk changes only; use data-driven risk scoring
CAB members don't prepare — reviews happen cold Distribute RFCs in advance with pre-read requirement
Low engagement — members see CAB as a waste of time Limit scope to genuinely risky changes; keep meetings focused and time-boxed
Scheduling conflicts — key members unavailable Rotate membership; allow asynchronous review for non-critical changes
Documentation overhead deters adoption Streamline RFC templates; automate data population from ITSM tools

Risk Assessment for Changes

Every non-standard change requires a risk assessment. The assessment considers:

Technical Risk: - Probability of failure during implementation - Complexity of the change (number of components affected) - Availability of rollback/back-out plan - Testing completeness (was it tested in a non-production environment?)

Business Risk: - User/customer impact if the change fails - Financial impact of downtime - Compliance or regulatory implications - Timing relative to business-critical periods

Security Risk: - Does the change introduce new attack surface? - Does it affect authentication, authorization, or encryption? - Has the Information Security Officer reviewed it?

Dependency Risk: - Are other changes dependent on this one? - Does this change depend on other changes completing first? - Are third-party vendors involved?

Risk scoring can be done through: - Simple High/Medium/Low classification - Numerical scoring matrices (probability x impact) - Automated risk scoring in ITSM tools (based on historical change success rates, configuration item criticality, etc.)

Change Schedule (Change Calendar)

The Change Schedule (called "Forward Schedule of Changes" or FSC in ITIL v2) is a calendar-based view of all planned and approved changes, along with key operational windows.

Contents: - Approved changes with planned implementation dates and times - Change type and risk level - Affected services and configuration items - Responsible team/implementer - Expected duration and downtime window - Dependencies between changes

Key scheduling concepts:

  • Maintenance Window: A pre-agreed time period during which changes may be implemented (e.g., Sundays 02:00-06:00). Reduces risk by scheduling changes during low-usage periods.
  • Blackout Window / Change Freeze: A period during which NO changes should be deployed. Used during high-risk business periods (e.g., end of academic term, enrollment periods, major events). Only emergency changes may proceed during a freeze.
  • Change Embargo: Similar to a blackout — a directive prohibiting specific types of changes during a defined period.

Practical guidance: - Make the change schedule visible to all IT teams and relevant business stakeholders - Schedule high-risk changes during maintenance windows only - Avoid scheduling multiple high-risk changes simultaneously - Consider business cycles and peak usage periods when scheduling - Emergency changes that occur outside the schedule must be logged retrospectively

How Security-Related Changes Are Handled

Security changes follow the same change enablement framework but have special considerations:

Security Patches

  • Routine/scheduled patches (monthly patch cycles): Typically handled as standard changes — pre-approved, follow documented procedures, automated where possible
  • Critical/zero-day patches: Handled as emergency changes — expedited through ECAB, mandatory PIR afterward
  • Key principle: Security patches should have pre-approved change models so routine patching does not bottleneck at CAB

Firewall Rule Changes

  • Adding standard application rules (well-documented, low risk): Can be standard changes if a pre-approved model exists
  • Modifying security zone boundaries or access policies: Normal change — requires risk assessment, security officer review, and appropriate authorization
  • Emergency rules to block an active attack: Emergency change through ECAB
  • Documentation: Must include requestor, business justification, exact rule/config changes, approval records, implementation time, verification results, and rollback plan

Access Control Changes

  • Standard user provisioning/deprovisioning: Standard change (follow established onboarding/offboarding procedures)
  • Privilege escalation requests: Normal change — requires manager approval and security review
  • Emergency access grants during incidents: Emergency change — time-limited, mandatory review after incident resolution
  • Policy changes affecting all users (e.g., new MFA requirement, password policy change): Major normal change requiring CAB review and business stakeholder communication

Coordinating Changes That Impact Everyone

Security policy changes that affect all users (e.g., new security awareness requirements, VPN policy changes, device management policies) require: 1. CAB review with business stakeholder representation 2. Communication plan — advance notice to all affected users 3. Phased rollout where possible — pilot group first, then organization-wide 4. Training/awareness — ensure users understand what is changing and why 5. Rollback plan — ability to revert if the change causes unexpected disruption 6. Change schedule visibility — all teams must know when the change takes effect

Relationship Between Change Enablement and Release Management

ITIL 4 separated these into distinct practices because they play different roles:

Aspect Change Enablement Release Management
Category Service Management Practice Service Management Practice
Focus Assessment, approval, and scheduling of changes Planning, building, testing, and deploying releases into production
When Before implementation — governance and authorization During implementation — execution and coordination
Key question "Should we make this change?" "How do we deploy this change safely?"
Output Authorized, scheduled change Successfully deployed release

How they interact: 1. Change Enablement evaluates and authorizes the change 2. Release Management plans the release (scope, components, timeline, resources) 3. Service Validation and Testing verifies release readiness 4. Deployment Management executes the technical deployment 5. Change Enablement conducts post-implementation review

Key principle: A successful change enablement process ensures that only approved, well-documented changes reach release management. This reduces the risk of unexpected disruptions during deployment.

Post-Implementation Review (PIR)

The PIR is a formal review conducted after a change has been implemented. It is MANDATORY for emergency changes and strongly recommended for major normal changes.

When to conduct: - Emergency changes: Always (typically within 48-72 hours) - Major normal changes: Always - Significant normal changes: Recommended - Minor normal / standard changes: Only if issues occurred during implementation

Who leads: Service Owner and Change Manager jointly

What the PIR covers: 1. Did the change achieve its objectives? — Were business goals met? 2. Was the implementation smooth? — Were there unexpected issues during deployment? 3. Impact assessment — Were there any negative effects on users, other services, or performance? 4. Stakeholder satisfaction — Are users and business stakeholders satisfied with the result? 5. Process evaluation — Did the change enablement process work effectively? Were approvals timely? Was communication adequate? 6. Lessons learned — What would we do differently next time? 7. Follow-up actions — Are there remaining issues, optimizations, or related changes needed?

PIR outcomes feed into: - Continual Improvement register - Updated change models and procedures - Known Error Database (if issues were found) - Risk assessment criteria (calibrate risk scoring based on actual outcomes) - CAB process improvements

Change Enablement KPIs

  • Change Success Rate: Percentage of changes implemented without causing incidents. Target: >95%
  • Change Volume by Type: Ratio of standard vs. normal vs. emergency changes. High emergency % indicates process problems.
  • Mean Change Lead Time: Average time from RFC submission to implementation. Shorter is better (but not at the expense of quality).
  • Failed Change Rate: Percentage of changes requiring rollback. Should trend downward.
  • Emergency Change Rate: Percentage of total changes classified as emergency. Should be <5-10%.
  • CAB Approval Time: Average time changes spend waiting for CAB review. Indicates bottleneck risk.
  • PIR Completion Rate: Percentage of emergency/major changes that complete PIR. Should be 100%.
  • Change-Related Incident Rate: Number of incidents traced back to recent changes. Core quality indicator.

Topic 6: Service Level Management (SLM)

Definition and Purpose

Service Level Management (SLM) is the ITIL 4 practice responsible for setting clear business-based targets for service levels, and for ensuring that delivery of services is properly assessed, monitored, and managed against these targets.

The purpose of SLM is to establish a shared understanding between the service provider and the service consumer about what service levels are expected, what is actually delivered, and what needs to improve.

Key principle: Focus on outcomes for the service consumer organization and on user experience more than on technical details and associated metrics.

SLA — Service Level Agreement

Formal definition: A documented agreement between a service provider and a customer that identifies both services required and the expected level of service.

Key characteristics: - Can be formal (legally binding contracts) or informal (internal agreements) - Must promote mutual understanding and be measurable - Should be regularly reviewed and updated - Written in plain, business-friendly language — avoid technical jargon - Focus on what matters to the customer, not what is easy for IT to measure

What a good SLA includes: - Service description and scope - Availability targets (e.g., 99.9% uptime during business hours) - Performance targets (e.g., response time under 2 seconds) - Incident response and resolution times by priority - Support hours and contact channels - Escalation procedures - Exclusions (what is NOT covered) - Review frequency and process - Consequences of breach (penalties, service credits, escalation)

SLA Types

ITIL v3 Types (for reference)

  1. Service-based SLA: One SLA covers one service for all customers (e.g., "Email Service SLA" applies to everyone)
  2. Customer-based SLA: One SLA covers all services for one customer (e.g., "Finance Department SLA" covering email, ERP, file shares)
  3. Multi-level SLA: Hybrid approach with corporate, customer, and service levels

ITIL 4 Simplified Types

  1. Tailored SLAs: Significant flexibility in target service levels, negotiated before service begins. Custom-designed for specific customer needs.
  2. Out-of-the-box SLAs: Pre-defined service levels with minimal customization. Standard offerings.

SLA vs OLA vs Underpinning Contract (UC)

Aspect SLA OLA UC
Full name Service Level Agreement Operational Level Agreement Underpinning Contract
Parties Service provider and customer Internal team to internal team Service provider and external supplier
Visibility Customer-facing Internal only Internal only
Legal status May or may not be legally binding Internal agreement, not legally binding Legally binding contract
Purpose Define service expectations for the customer Define internal team responsibilities that support the SLA Define external supplier obligations that support the SLA
Example "IT will resolve P1 incidents within 1 hour" "Network team will diagnose network-related P1 incidents within 20 minutes" "ISP will restore connectivity within 30 minutes of reported outage"

ITIL 4 simplification: ITIL 4 dropped the OLA and UC terminology because in practice, everyone calls everything an SLA. The distinction exists only in your perspective — whether you are the provider or consumer of a service. ITIL 4 recognizes this reality and uses "SLA" universally, with the understanding that supporting agreements must align with and not conflict with the primary customer-facing SLA.

Practical caution: Even without the formal OLA/UC labels, organizations MUST ensure that internal team targets and external supplier commitments support the customer-facing SLA. A classic failure: promising 4-hour resolution to the customer when the external vendor's contract allows 8-hour response.

These are illustrative — each organization defines targets based on its business needs, risk tolerance, and capacity.

Response Time vs Resolution Time: - Response Time: How quickly the service provider acknowledges the issue and begins working on it (first human response, not an auto-acknowledgment) - Resolution Time: How quickly the issue is fully resolved and service restored to normal operation

Internal SLAs are critical because: They are the building blocks that support external SLAs. If internal teams do not have clear, measurable targets, external commitments become aspirational rather than achievable.

SLA Monitoring and Reporting

Monitoring approaches: - Real-time dashboards: Live views of SLA compliance across all services and priorities - Automated alerts: Notifications when SLA targets are approaching breach (e.g., at 75% and 90% of allowed time) - Periodic reports: Weekly/monthly summaries of SLA performance for management review

What to monitor: - Response time compliance by priority level - Resolution time compliance by priority level - Availability metrics vs. targets - Incident volume trends - Customer satisfaction scores (CSAT) - Reopened ticket rates (indicates resolution quality)

Service Review Meetings: - Regular meetings between service provider and customer to review SLA performance - Recommended frequency: Monthly (maximum interval: quarterly) - Can also be triggered by events: major incidents, significant changes, altered business requirements - Agenda: Performance data, trends, breaches, improvement actions, upcoming changes

What Happens When SLAs Are Breached

An SLA breach occurs when the service provider fails to meet the agreed-upon performance targets.

Immediate response: 1. Acknowledge the breach promptly — demonstrate commitment to resolution 2. Escalate per escalation procedures — notify management, activate additional resources 3. Communicate transparently — inform the customer about what happened, what is being done, and expected timeline

Formal consequences (vary by agreement): - Service credits: Customer receives a credit on their next invoice proportional to the breach severity - Financial penalties: Pre-defined monetary penalties for breach of specific targets - Escalation procedures: Breach triggers management involvement and oversight - Improvement plans: Service provider must present a formal remediation and prevention plan - Contract review: Repeated or severe breaches may trigger contract renegotiation or termination rights

Root cause analysis: - Every significant SLA breach should trigger root cause investigation - Determine: Was the breach due to process failure, resource constraints, external factors, or unrealistic targets? - Document findings and implement preventive measures - Update SLA targets if analysis reveals they were unrealistic

Key principle: SLA breach management should focus on improvement, not blame. The goal is to understand why the breach happened and prevent recurrence.

The "Watermelon SLA" Effect

Definition: The watermelon effect occurs when SLA metrics appear green (all targets met) on the outside, but the customer's actual experience is red (dissatisfied) on the inside — just like cutting open a watermelon.

Why it happens: - SLAs measure what is easy to quantify (uptime, response time) rather than what users actually experience - Narrow focus on operational metrics misses solution quality, ease of access, and user effort - No feedback loops — teams never hear from frustrated users - Isolated accountability — no one owns overall user experience

Real-world examples:

SLA Metric (Green) User Reality (Red) Root Cause
95% of tickets resolved within 24 hours User satisfaction only 5/10 Fast closure prioritized over solution quality
99.9% uptime achieved Daily frustrations with slow VPN, WiFi drops Infrastructure technically "available" but functionally inadequate
Quick response times documented Employees find self-service options difficult and struggle to reach humans Metrics ignore user effort required to resolve issues
99.9% availability of telecom lines Customer unhappy — downtime hits during peak business hours The 0.1% downtime coincides with highest-traffic periods

How to detect watermelon SLAs: - SLA dashboards show green while user surveys show dissatisfaction - Same issues repeatedly submitted despite SLA compliance - Users stop submitting tickets, accepting poor workarounds instead - Low Net Promoter Score despite met SLA targets - Declining productivity or employee complaints about IT tools

The fix: Experience Level Agreements (XLAs)

XLAs complement SLAs by measuring how users perceive the service, not just how the service performs technically.

Metric SLA Focus XLA Focus
Response time Fast acknowledgment User satisfaction with response quality
Resolution time Quick closure User perception of solution effectiveness
Uptime System availability System performance from user perspective
Support access Channel availability Ease of reaching help

Practical XLA metrics (pick 3-5 maximum): - User satisfaction rating after each support interaction - Ease-of-resolution score (1-5 scale) - First-contact resolution rate - Time spent on issue from user's perspective - Willingness to use self-service options - Employee Satisfaction Score (ESAT) targeting specific pain points

Implementation approach: 1. Survey users to identify their biggest IT frustrations 2. Define XLA metrics that capture those frustrations 3. Implement real-time dashboards tracking both SLAs and XLAs side by side 4. Assign specific ownership for experience improvement 5. Include XLA data in management reporting alongside SLA data 6. Review monthly and act on insights — fix recurring pain points

Service Level Management KPIs

  • SLA Compliance Rate: Percentage of service targets met across all SLAs
  • Customer Satisfaction (CSAT/XLA): User-reported satisfaction with service delivery
  • Service Availability: Actual availability vs. committed targets
  • Breach Count and Trend: Number and direction of SLA breaches over time
  • SLA Review Completion: Percentage of SLAs reviewed on schedule
  • Time to Resolve by Priority: Actual resolution times vs. SLA targets by priority level

Topic 7: Information Security Management (ISM)

Definition and Purpose

Information Security Management in ITIL 4 is a general management practice (not a service management practice) responsible for protecting the information needed by the organization to conduct its business. This classification as "general management" reflects that security is an organization-wide concern, not limited to IT service delivery.

Purpose: To protect the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of information — the CIA Triad: - Confidentiality: Only authorized people can access the information - Integrity: Information is accurate, complete, and has not been improperly modified - Availability: Information is accessible to authorized people when they need it

Key philosophy: Security is not something you add at the end — it needs to be woven into the fabric of everything you do from the outset. Security is everyone's responsibility, not just the security team's.

Security as a Cross-Cutting Concern

Unlike most ITIL practices that are invoked situationally (e.g., incident management is triggered by an incident), information security management operates continuously across the entire IT service value system. It is embedded throughout:

  • Plan: Security requirements inform strategy; regulatory obligations shape priorities
  • Improve: Security enhancements are part of continual improvement; blameless retrospectives examine security incidents
  • Engage: Security communication to stakeholders; security awareness training
  • Design & Transition: Security considerations from design inception; secure coding standards; threat modeling
  • Obtain/Build: Security testing in CI/CD pipelines; vulnerability scanning; container security
  • Deliver & Support: Monitoring for security events; incident response; access control enforcement

This is what makes security "cross-cutting": It does not live in one box on the org chart. It influences and constrains every other practice, every value chain activity, and every team. An organization cannot be excellent at incident management, change enablement, or service delivery if it is poor at security — because a security failure undermines all of them.

The Protect-Detect-Correct Framework

ITIL 4 advocates a three-pillar security approach:

1. Prevention (Protect): - Proactive measures preventing incidents before they occur - Securing network devices, centralizing firewalls - User training and awareness — users are the biggest area of exposure - Access control policies, least-privilege principles - Encryption of data at rest and in transit - Secure configuration management

2. Detection (Detect): - Rapid identification of security events and incidents - Antivirus, anti-malware, intrusion detection/prevention systems - SIEM (Security Information and Event Management) tools - Continuous monitoring and alerting - Log aggregation and correlation - Vulnerability scanning

3. Correction (Correct): - Recovery from security incidents - Incident response and containment - Root cause analysis and lessons learned - Network auditing and forensic analysis - Policy and procedure updates based on incident findings

Security Maturity Levels

Level Name Characteristics
1 Ad-hoc Reactive, unplanned responses; no formal policies; security is an afterthought
2 Basic Initial policies established; limited integration with IT operations; awareness emerging
3 Structured Formal procedures in place; regular risk assessments; security training programs
4 Managed Full integration between security and IT operations; continuous monitoring; advanced incident response
5 Optimized Business-driven security strategy; proactive threat anticipation; security culture fully embedded

Coordination Between Security Team and Other IT Teams

This is one of the most practical aspects of ISM — how to prevent the security team from "stepping on everyone's feet" while still maintaining protection.

The Traditional Problem

In many organizations, the security team operates as external gatekeepers who: - Say "no" without understanding business context - Impose blanket controls that frustrate operations teams - Create bottlenecks in change and deployment processes - Are seen as adversaries rather than collaborators - Make rules that other teams do not understand or follow

ITIL 4's Solution: Security as Enabler, Not Gatekeeper

Role transformation: The Security Officer's role shifts from specifying requirements and monitoring performance to enabling practitioners to address security concerns themselves. Security professionals become trusted advisors, not adversaries.

Practical coordination mechanisms:

  1. Embed security in daily operations: Involve all teams in building security practices into day-to-day activities. When security is part of everyone's job, it stops being an external constraint imposed by a separate team.

  2. Security representation on CAB: An Information Security Officer should be a standing member of the CAB, providing security perspective on all significant changes without being a separate gate.

  3. Collaborative risk assessment: Use threat modeling (e.g., STRIDE methodology) to identify genuine threats versus perceived ones. Security teams should "pick their battles" — evaluate assets against specific vulnerabilities rather than applying blanket controls everywhere.

  4. Shared change enablement process: When the security team needs to change IT infrastructure to enhance security (firewall rules, access policies, encryption standards), those changes go through the same change enablement process as any other change. Security submits RFCs, provides risk assessments, and follows the same scheduling and approval workflow. This ensures visibility and prevents unilateral changes that disrupt other teams.

  5. Integrated incident response: Security incidents use the same incident management framework with additional security-specific protocols. The security team does not run a separate incident process — they plug into the existing one with enhanced procedures.

  6. Knowledge sharing: Security policies must be accessible and understandable to all practitioners. Avoid jargon-heavy policy documents that no one reads.

  7. Cross-training: Train operations staff on basic security principles; train security staff on operational realities and business context. This mutual understanding reduces friction.

Security Changes and Their Impact on Everyone

Security changes often affect the entire organization (unlike most IT changes that affect specific services). ITIL handles this through:

CAB Involvement in Security Decisions

  • Routine security patches: Pre-approved as standard changes; no CAB needed
  • Security policy changes affecting all users (MFA rollout, password policy, device management): Major normal change requiring full CAB review with business stakeholder representation
  • Network security changes (firewall zone restructuring, VPN policy changes): Normal change with security officer and network team co-assessment
  • Emergency security changes (blocking an active attack, zero-day patches): ECAB authorization with mandatory PIR
  • Compliance-driven changes (responding to audit findings, regulatory requirements): Normal change with compliance and security review

The Coordination Challenge

Security changes are unique because they often: - Affect every user simultaneously (unlike most IT changes that affect specific services) - Require behavior change from users (not just technical changes) - Have compliance implications that cannot be negotiated - Create tension between security and usability — making something more secure often makes it less convenient

ITIL's framework for managing this tension: 1. Use change enablement to assess and schedule security changes like any other change 2. Require business stakeholder representation when security changes impact user experience 3. Phase rollouts where possible (pilot group, then department, then organization-wide) 4. Communicate in advance — explain what is changing, why, and what users need to do 5. Provide training and support resources before the change takes effect 6. Monitor impact after implementation — both technical metrics and user experience

Information Security Incident Management

Security incidents require a different handling approach compared to regular IT incidents:

How Security Incidents Differ from Regular Incidents

Aspect Regular Incident Security Incident
Goal Restore service ASAP Contain threat, preserve evidence, THEN restore service
Evidence Documentation for service improvement Forensic evidence preservation for investigation/legal
Communication Standard stakeholder notification Controlled disclosure; potential regulatory notification
Escalation Functional/hierarchical within IT May involve legal, compliance, law enforcement, regulators
Scope Usually affects specific service May have organization-wide implications
Timeline Restore quickly, close ticket Investigation may continue long after service restoration
Containment Optional (fix the problem) Mandatory (isolate affected systems before restoration)

Security Incident Response Process (ITIL 4)

  1. Preparation (ongoing, before any incident):
  2. Establish incident response policies and procedures
  3. Define communication plans and escalation paths
  4. Identify business-critical services and their security requirements
  5. Maintain "break glass" emergency response plans

  6. Conduct tabletop exercises and fire drills

  7. Detection and Escalation:

  8. Monitoring tools and SIEM detect potential security events
  9. Security events are filtered and correlated to identify actual incidents
  10. Confirmed security incidents are logged and immediately escalated to the security response team

  11. Initial severity classification

  12. Triage and Analysis:

  13. Forensic data collection — logs, system images, network captures
  14. Determine scope of compromise
  15. Identify attack vector and threat actor (if possible)

  16. Assess business impact

  17. Containment and Recovery:

  18. Isolate affected systems to prevent spread
  19. Eradicate the threat (remove malware, close vulnerabilities, revoke compromised credentials)
  20. Restore affected services from known-good state

  21. Verify restoration is clean (no persistent threat)

  22. Post-Incident Activity:

  23. Root cause analysis — how did the breach occur?
  24. Lessons learned — what could have been detected sooner?
  25. Policy and procedure updates
  26. Insurance notification if applicable
  27. Regulatory notification if required (data breach notification laws)
  28. Update security controls, monitoring rules, and training based on findings
  29. Feed into continual improvement

Balancing Security Requirements with Service Delivery

The fundamental tension: Security measures that are too strict impede service delivery and frustrate users; security measures that are too lax expose the organization to risk.

ITIL 4's balancing principles:

  1. Risk-based approach: Not everything needs the same level of protection. Classify information and systems by criticality, and apply controls proportional to the risk. A public-facing website does not need the same access controls as the payroll system.

  2. Security as a quality attribute: Think of security like warranty — it is part of what makes a service "fit for use." Adequate security enables service consumption; inadequate security undermines it.

  3. Involve security early: Include security requirements in service design from the start (shift-left security). Retrofitting security after design is more expensive and more disruptive than designing it in.

  4. Automate security controls: Automated security checks in CI/CD pipelines, automated credential management, automated compliance scanning — these provide security without creating manual bottlenecks.

  5. Measure the right things: Track security metrics that matter to the business (time to detect threats, time to contain incidents, compliance posture) rather than vanity metrics (number of firewall rules, number of vulnerabilities scanned).

  6. Accept residual risk consciously: Not all risk can be eliminated. The organization should formally accept residual risk through documented risk acceptance, not ignore it or pretend it does not exist.

How ITIL Prevents "Stepping on Each Other's Feet"

The core mechanisms ITIL provides to prevent conflict between security and operations:

  1. Shared change process: Both teams use the same change enablement practice. Security does not make unilateral changes; operations does not bypass security review. All changes go through the same risk assessment and authorization workflow.

  2. Standing security representation: Security officer on the CAB ensures security perspective is heard during change review without requiring a separate security gate.

  3. Clear RACI matrix: Defined roles for security in every practice — who is Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, and Informed. This prevents ambiguity about who decides what.

  4. Integrated incident management: One incident process with security-specific extensions, not two competing processes. When a security incident occurs, the security team plugs into the existing incident management framework with additional protocols.

  5. Shared visibility: Change schedule, incident dashboards, and service catalogs are visible to both security and operations teams. No one is surprised by what the other team is doing.

  6. Common language: ITIL provides terminology and concepts that both teams understand, reducing miscommunication and misaligned expectations.

  7. Continual improvement: Regular joint reviews of how security and operations interact, with specific improvement actions tracked and implemented.

Information Security Management KPIs

  • Mean Time to Detect (MTTD): Average time between a security event occurring and being detected. Lower is better.
  • Mean Time to Contain (MTTC): Average time from detection to containment. Measures incident response speed.
  • Security Incident Volume: Number of security incidents per period. Track trends, not absolute numbers.
  • Patch Compliance Rate: Percentage of systems with current security patches. Target: >95%.
  • Security Training Completion: Percentage of staff who have completed security awareness training.
  • Vulnerability Remediation Time: Average time to remediate identified vulnerabilities by severity.
  • Compliance Audit Findings: Number and severity of findings from security audits.
  • False Positive Rate: Percentage of security alerts that turn out to be non-issues. High rates indicate monitoring tuning needed.
  • Change-Related Security Incidents: Security incidents caused by changes. Indicates quality of security review in change enablement.

Topic 8: Supplier Management

Classification and Purpose

Category: General Management Practice (one of the 14 general management practices in ITIL 4)

Purpose: To ensure that the organization's suppliers and their performances are managed appropriately to support the seamless provision of quality products and services. The practice ensures that all contracts with suppliers support business needs, and that all suppliers meet their contractual commitments.

Why it matters for KU: With dependencies on Diyar (SI partner), BMC, Google, Microsoft, and multiple contractors, a structured approach to supplier management is essential. The current situation with BMC Helix (3 years deployed, underutilized, end-of-April deadline) is a textbook case for why formal supplier management matters.

Supplier Categorization

ITIL defines four categories of suppliers, each requiring different levels of management attention and resource investment:

1. Strategic Suppliers

  • What they are: Suppliers involving senior management and significant resources. They provide services that are critical to the organization's ability to deliver its own services.
  • Management approach: Close partnership, regular executive-level reviews, joint planning, shared risks and rewards
  • Relationship model: Deep integration, dedicated relationship managers, strategic alignment meetings
  • Contract type: Complex, long-term agreements with detailed governance frameworks
  • KU examples: BMC (ITSM platform), Microsoft (O365, directory services), potentially Google (Cloud Platform)

2. Tactical Suppliers

  • What they are: Suppliers involving significant commercial activity and business interaction. They provide important services or components but are not as deeply integrated as strategic suppliers.
  • Management approach: Regular business reviews, performance monitoring, defined escalation paths
  • Relationship model: Formal relationship management with periodic reviews
  • Contract type: Medium-term contracts with clear deliverables and milestones
  • KU examples: Diyar (system integrator), specific consultancy firms

3. Operational Suppliers

  • What they are: Suppliers providing operational products or services. They are important but relatively straightforward to manage and replace.
  • Management approach: Standard contract management, periodic performance reviews, routine monitoring
  • Relationship model: Transactional with defined touchpoints
  • Contract type: Standard commercial agreements with SLAs
  • KU examples: Hardware vendors, cabling contractors, print services

4. Commodity Suppliers

  • What they are: Suppliers providing low-value, easily sourced products or services. Minimal management effort required.
  • Management approach: Automated ordering, minimal oversight, price-driven selection
  • Relationship model: Purely transactional
  • Contract type: Standard purchase orders, catalog-based procurement
  • KU examples: Office supplies, consumables, basic accessories

Contract Management

Contract management is a core activity within supplier management. It covers the entire contract lifecycle:

Contract Lifecycle Stages

  1. Identification of Need: Business requirements drive supplier engagement. The need must be clearly articulated before procurement begins.

  2. Evaluation and Selection: Prospective suppliers are assessed against criteria including:

  3. Technical capability and expertise
  4. Financial stability and viability
  5. References and track record
  6. Cultural fit and willingness to partner
  7. Compliance with organizational policies and regulations

  8. Cost-effectiveness (not just lowest cost)

  9. Contract Negotiation and Establishment: Formal agreements are negotiated covering:

  10. Scope of services/products
  11. Service levels and performance targets
  12. Pricing and payment terms
  13. Intellectual property rights
  14. Confidentiality and data protection
  15. Termination and exit clauses
  16. Dispute resolution mechanisms

  17. Change management procedures for the contract itself

  18. Contract Execution and Monitoring: Ongoing management during the contract term:

  19. Regular performance reviews against agreed metrics
  20. Tracking of deliverables and milestones
  21. Issue and risk management
  22. Relationship management activities

  23. Financial tracking and invoice verification

  24. Renewal or Termination: End-of-term assessment:

  25. Performance review against original objectives
  26. Market comparison (are better alternatives available?)
  27. Renegotiation of terms if renewing
  28. Transition planning if terminating
  29. Knowledge transfer requirements

Key Contract Types in ITIL

  • Underpinning Contract (UC): A contract between an IT service provider and a third party (external supplier). The third party provides goods or services that support the IT service provider in delivering services to customers. UCs are the supplier-side equivalent of SLAs — they "underpin" the customer-facing SLA.

  • Operational Level Agreement (OLA): An internal agreement between IT groups within the same organization (not a supplier contract, but closely related). OLAs define internal service targets that support the external SLA.

  • Service Level Agreement (SLA): The customer-facing agreement. SLAs depend on both OLAs (internal teams) and UCs (external suppliers) being met.

The SLA-OLA-UC Relationship Chain:

Customer <-- SLA --> IT Service Provider <-- OLA --> Internal IT Teams
                                          <-- UC  --> External Suppliers

Both OLAs and UCs must support the commitments made in the SLA. If a UC allows a supplier 6 hours for response, but the SLA promises the customer 4 hours, there is a misalignment that will cause SLA breaches.

Underpinning Contracts (UCs) in Detail

Underpinning Contracts are the mechanism for ensuring external suppliers support customer SLA commitments. Key characteristics:

  • Alignment requirement: UC targets must be equal to or tighter than the SLA targets they support. If your SLA promises 99.9% uptime, the UC must guarantee at least 99.9% from the supplier's component.
  • Penalty clauses: UCs should include financial or service remedies when suppliers fail to meet agreed targets — liquidated damages, service credits, or escalation procedures.
  • Performance reporting: Suppliers must provide regular reports demonstrating compliance with UC targets.
  • Review schedule: UCs should be reviewed regularly (quarterly or annually) to ensure they remain aligned with evolving SLAs and business needs.
  • Exit provisions: UCs must include clear exit/termination clauses to avoid vendor lock-in, including data migration, knowledge transfer, and transition support obligations.

Vendor Performance Measurement

Effective supplier management requires systematic performance measurement:

What to Measure

  1. Service Level Achievement: Are the agreed service levels being met? Track each metric in the UC and compare actual vs. target.

  2. Quality of Deliverables: Are products/services delivered to the agreed quality standards? Measure defect rates, rework frequency, and user satisfaction.

  3. Responsiveness: How quickly does the supplier respond to requests, incidents, and escalations? Track response and resolution times.

  4. Proactiveness: Does the supplier merely react, or do they proactively identify improvements, risks, and opportunities?

  5. Innovation and Value Add: Is the supplier contributing ideas for improvement, bringing new capabilities, and helping the organization evolve?

  6. Financial Performance: Is the supplier delivering within budget? Are costs reasonable compared to market benchmarks?

  7. Relationship Quality: How effective is communication? Are escalations handled professionally? Is there mutual trust?

  8. Compliance: Is the supplier complying with regulatory requirements, security policies, and contractual terms?

Performance Measurement Tools

  • Supplier Scorecards: Weighted scoring across multiple dimensions (delivery, quality, cost, relationship, innovation). Updated quarterly.
  • Service Level Reports: Monthly or quarterly reports comparing actual performance against UC targets.
  • Customer/User Satisfaction Surveys: Feedback from the people who interact with or depend on the supplier's services.
  • Contract Review Meetings: Formal meetings (monthly, quarterly) with documented minutes and action items.
  • Supplier & Contract Management Information System (SCMIS): A centralized database or tool for tracking all supplier information, contracts, performance data, and relationship history. Integrated into the ITIL Service Knowledge Management System (SKMS).

Multi-Supplier Environments

Most organizations use multiple suppliers, creating integration challenges:

Challenges

  • Different suppliers have different processes, tools, and communication styles
  • No single supplier has end-to-end visibility of the service
  • Finger-pointing when incidents occur ("not our fault")
  • Complex dependency chains — one supplier's failure cascades to others
  • Inconsistent reporting formats and metrics
  • Cultural and timezone differences

Service Integration and Management (SIAM)

SIAM is the management methodology specifically designed for multi-supplier environments:

  • Service Integrator Role: An entity (internal team or external provider) that acts as the single point of accountability for end-to-end service delivery across all suppliers.
  • Integration Layer: Common processes, tools, and governance that all suppliers must follow, regardless of their internal practices.
  • Cross-Supplier Coordination: Defined procedures for how suppliers interact with each other during incidents, changes, and service requests.
  • End-to-End Reporting: The service integrator consolidates performance data from all suppliers into unified reports.

SIAM Models

  1. Internal Integrator: Organization manages integration itself (requires significant internal capability)
  2. External Integrator: A dedicated third party manages integration (adds cost but provides expertise)
  3. Hybrid: Mix of internal and external integration capabilities
  4. Lead Supplier: One supplier acts as integrator for others (risk of bias)

Holding Contractors Accountable Through ITIL Processes

Practical mechanisms for contractor accountability:

  1. Formal UCs with Measurable Targets: Every contractor engagement must have documented, measurable service levels — not vague statements like "best efforts" but specific targets like "P1 incident response within 30 minutes, resolution within 4 hours."

  2. Regular Performance Reviews: Schedule and enforce regular review meetings (monthly or quarterly). Document findings and required corrective actions. Track action items to completion.

  3. Escalation Procedures: Define clear escalation paths — who to contact when the contractor isn't performing, what happens at each escalation level, and what authority each level has (including contract termination authority).

  4. Integration with Change Enablement: Require contractors to follow the organization's change management process. All changes must be approved, tested, and documented through the standard change process. This prevents contractors from making unauthorized changes.

  5. Integration with Incident Management: Require contractors to use the organization's incident management system. Track contractor-related incidents, response times, and resolution quality. Use this data in performance reviews.

  6. Knowledge Transfer Requirements: Build knowledge transfer obligations into contracts. Contractors must document their work, create runbooks, and train internal staff. This prevents dependency and ensures the organization can function without the contractor.

  7. Financial Penalties and Incentives: Include both penalties for underperformance (service credits, liquidated damages) and incentives for exceptional performance (contract extensions, expanded scope, bonus payments).

  8. Right-to-Audit Clauses: Include contractual rights to audit the contractor's processes, tools, and deliverables. This ensures transparency and accountability.

  9. Exit Management Planning: Every contractor engagement should have a documented exit plan from day one — what happens when the contract ends, how knowledge is transferred, how data is migrated, and how transition is managed.

KU Relevance — Current Supplier Challenges

The BMC Helix situation illustrates several supplier management failures: - Misaligned UCs/SLAs: No clear performance targets for BMC deployment progress - Contractor dependency: Diyar (SI) required for all BMC configuration — internal team cannot self-serve - Knowledge transfer gap: 3 years in, internal team still can't configure BMC without Diyar - Weak accountability: No formal performance scorecards tracking BMC/Diyar delivery - No exit planning: Until the RFP4/ServiceNow lever emerged, there was no alternative path

Topic 9: Financial Management of IT (Service Financial Management)

Classification and Purpose

Category: General Management Practice (one of the 14 general management practices in ITIL 4)

Purpose: To support the organization's strategies and plans for service management by ensuring that the organization's financial resources and investments are being used effectively. The practice covers budgeting, accounting, and charging for IT services.

ITIL 4 name: "Service financial management" (changed from "Financial management for IT services" in ITIL v3)

Why it matters for KU: Understanding the true cost of IT services enables informed decision-making about insource vs. outsource, cloud vs. on-premises, and the value IT delivers to the university. It also supports the AVP Office's budget transparency initiative and helps justify investments to university leadership.

The Three Core Activities

1. Budgeting

Budgeting is the process of predicting and controlling income and expenditure for IT services.

Types of IT budgets: - Capital Budget (CapEx): Major purchases and investments — servers, network equipment, software licenses with multi-year terms, data center buildouts. These are capitalized on the balance sheet and depreciated over their useful life. - Operational Budget (OpEx): Day-to-day running costs — salaries, cloud subscriptions, maintenance contracts, consumables, utilities. These are expensed in the period they occur. - Project Budget: Specific budget allocations for defined projects with a beginning and end — system implementations, migrations, infrastructure upgrades.

Budget planning cycle: 1. Strategic planning: Align IT budget with organizational strategy (3-5 year horizon) 2. Demand forecasting: Estimate future service demand and required capacity 3. Resource planning: Determine staffing, infrastructure, and vendor costs needed 4. Budget submission: Present budget to governance/finance for approval 5. Allocation: Distribute approved budget across services, departments, and projects 6. Monitoring: Track actual spend against budget throughout the year 7. Variance analysis: Explain and address deviations from budget 8. Reporting: Regular financial reports to stakeholders

2. Accounting

IT accounting tracks and reports how IT money is actually spent. It provides the data foundation for budgeting and charging decisions.

Cost categorization — multiple dimensions:

By relationship to service: - Direct costs: Costs that can be directly attributed to a specific service (e.g., a server that only runs one application) - Indirect costs: Costs shared across multiple services that must be allocated using some formula (e.g., network infrastructure, shared storage, management overhead)

By behavior: - Fixed costs: Costs that remain constant regardless of service volume (e.g., annual license fees, salaried staff, facility lease) - Variable costs: Costs that change with service volume (e.g., cloud computing usage, per-transaction fees, overtime)

By nature: - Capital expenditure (CapEx): One-time purchases of long-lived assets (hardware, perpetual licenses, construction) - Operational expenditure (OpEx): Recurring costs of running services (subscriptions, salaries, utilities, maintenance)

Cost models: - Absorption costing: Allocates all costs (direct and indirect) to services. Every cost in the IT organization is attributed to a service. - Activity-based costing (ABC): Allocates costs based on activities that drive costs. More accurate but more complex to implement. - Marginal costing: Only considers incremental costs of providing one more unit of service. Useful for pricing decisions.

3. Charging

Charging (or billing) is the process of assigning costs for IT services to the business units or customers who consume them. Not all organizations charge — it is a policy decision.

Charging models:

Model Description Pros Cons
Chargeback Business units are formally billed for their actual IT service consumption. Real money transfers between internal accounts. Creates cost awareness, drives responsible consumption, treats IT as a business Can be complex to implement, may create adversarial relationships, overhead of billing system
Showback Business units are shown what their IT service consumption costs, but no actual billing occurs. IT remains centrally funded. Creates cost awareness without billing complexity, educational, non-threatening No financial incentive to reduce consumption, "information only" can be ignored
Tiered/Differential Charging Different rates for different service levels (e.g., gold/silver/bronze). Users choose the level they need and can afford. Demand management, self-selection, aligns cost with value Complex pricing structure, may drive users to lowest tier even when inappropriate
Notional Charging Shadow billing — charges are calculated and reported but never collected. Used as a trial run before implementing real chargeback. Low-risk way to test charging, identifies issues before real billing Lacks financial consequence, may not change behavior

Total Cost of Ownership (TCO)

TCO calculates the full lifecycle cost of a service or asset, including all hidden and indirect costs.

TCO components:

  1. Acquisition costs: Purchase price, licensing fees, implementation costs, migration costs, training costs, consultancy fees for initial deployment
  2. Operating costs: Annual maintenance and support, cloud subscriptions, staffing costs (FTEs and contractors), energy and facilities, monitoring and management tools
  3. Enhancement costs: Upgrades, patches, additional modules, capacity increases, integration with new systems
  4. Retirement costs: Decommissioning, data migration to replacement system, contract termination fees, disposal of hardware, knowledge transfer
  5. Hidden costs: Productivity loss during transitions, opportunity cost (what else could we do with these resources?), risk mitigation costs (DR, security), compliance costs (audits, certifications)

TCO calculation enables: - Fair comparison between alternatives (e.g., BMC Helix vs. ServiceNow — total cost over 5 years, not just license price) - Understanding the true cost of "free" open-source solutions (operational cost is not zero) - Identifying the break-even point for cloud migration decisions - Building business cases with credible financial projections

Service Costing

Service costing determines what each IT service costs to deliver. This is foundational — you cannot do chargeback, showback, TCO analysis, or business value conversations without knowing your service costs.

Steps to build a service cost model:

  1. Define the service catalog: List all IT services delivered to the business
  2. Identify cost elements: For each service, identify all direct costs (dedicated resources) and allocate indirect costs (shared resources)
  3. Determine allocation methods: How are shared costs divided? Options include:
  4. Per user/per device (simple but imprecise)
  5. By consumption (accurate but requires metering)
  6. By revenue/headcount of consuming business unit (proxy-based)
  7. By activity (most accurate, most complex)
  8. Calculate unit costs: Cost per user, cost per transaction, cost per GB, etc.
  9. Validate and refine: Compare calculated costs with actual spend; adjust allocation methods to improve accuracy
  10. Communicate: Share service costs with stakeholders and use them for decision-making

Linking IT Spending to Business Value

The central challenge of IT financial management: demonstrating that IT spending creates business value, not just cost.

Approaches:

  1. Value-Based Budgeting: Instead of justifying IT budget as a percentage of revenue or by line-item cost, justify it by the business outcomes IT enables. "This ERP investment reduces procurement cycle time by 40%, saving $X per year" rather than "we need $Y for ERP licenses."

  2. Service Portfolio Analysis: Map IT services to business capabilities. Identify which services are:

  3. Run (keeping the lights on — necessary but not differentiating)
  4. Grow (enhancing existing capabilities — competitive advantage)

  5. Transform (creating new capabilities — innovation) Track spending across these categories to ensure appropriate investment balance.

  6. Business Outcome Metrics: Define IT metrics in business terms:

  7. Instead of "99.9% uptime" → "student registration system available during all registration periods with zero data loss"

  8. Instead of "average resolution time 4 hours" → "faculty can resume teaching within 4 hours of any IT disruption"

  9. IT as a Value Center (not Cost Center): Reframe IT from "what does it cost?" to "what does it enable?" This requires:

  10. Revenue attribution (what revenue does IT-enabled service X generate?)
  11. Cost avoidance (what would it cost if IT didn't provide this service?)
  12. Risk reduction (what is the financial impact of risks that IT mitigates?)
  13. Productivity gains (how much staff time does IT automation save?)

Key Metrics for IT Financial Management

  • IT spend as percentage of revenue/budget: Benchmarking metric (varies by industry; higher education typically 4-7%)
  • Cost per user: Total IT cost divided by number of users served
  • Cost per service: Total cost of delivering each defined service
  • Run/Grow/Transform ratio: What percentage of IT budget goes to each category
  • Budget variance: Actual spend vs. planned budget (target: within 5%)
  • Return on IT investment (ROI): Financial return generated by IT investments
  • Unit cost trends: Are costs per unit of service declining over time (efficiency)?

Topic 10: Knowledge Management

Classification and Purpose

Category: General Management Practice (one of the 14 general management practices in ITIL 4)

Purpose: To maintain and improve the effective, efficient, and convenient use of information and knowledge across the organization. The practice ensures that stakeholders get the right information, in the right format, at the right level, at the right time.

Core principle: The power of knowledge lies in its use and exploitation, not merely in its management. Knowledge that is captured but never used is waste.

The DIKW Model (Data → Information → Knowledge → Wisdom)

The DIKW model is the foundational framework for understanding knowledge management in ITIL. It describes four levels of understanding, each building on the level below:

1. Data (Raw Facts)

  • Definition: Discrete, objective facts about events. Data has no meaning on its own without context.
  • Characteristics: Raw, unprocessed, unorganized, context-free
  • Examples in ITSM:
  • "Server CPU utilization: 87%"
  • "Incident ticket #4521 opened at 14:32"
  • "Network switch port 24 status: down"
  • "3,847 emails processed today"
  • Management task: Capture accurately, store reliably, ensure integrity

2. Information (Data with Context)

  • Definition: Data that has been processed, organized, and given context so that it becomes meaningful.
  • Transformation: Data becomes information when we add context (who, what, when, where, why)
  • Examples in ITSM:
  • "Web server CPU has been above 85% for 3 hours, which is above the 80% threshold"
  • "Incident #4521 was a P2 network outage affecting the registration building, opened by Dr. Ahmad"
  • "Email volume increased 47% compared to last Monday due to registration deadline"
  • Management task: Organize, categorize, present in meaningful format

3. Knowledge (Information with Experience and Judgment)

  • Definition: Information combined with experience, context, interpretation, and reflection. Knowledge enables people to make decisions and take action.
  • Transformation: Information becomes knowledge when people apply experience, training, and judgment to interpret it
  • Types:
  • Tacit knowledge: Personal, context-specific, hard to formalize — "I know from experience that this error usually means the database connection pool is exhausted"
  • Explicit knowledge: Codified, documented, easily shared — runbooks, procedures, known error records
  • Examples in ITSM:
  • "When CPU stays above 85% for more than 2 hours on this server, it typically means the batch job is stuck. Restarting the job queue resolves it without affecting users."
  • "This type of network issue is always caused by the same firmware bug. Apply patch KB-2847."
  • Management task: Capture (especially tacit → explicit), organize, make accessible, keep current

4. Wisdom (Knowledge Applied with Judgment)

  • Definition: The ability to make sound judgments and decisions based on knowledge, experience, and understanding of context. Wisdom involves understanding the "why" behind things.
  • Transformation: Knowledge becomes wisdom through reflection, experience across multiple contexts, and understanding of consequences
  • Examples in ITSM:
  • "Given our university's registration cycles, we should proactively scale server capacity two weeks before each registration period, not wait for alerts"
  • "This vendor always promises aggressive timelines. Based on three previous projects, add 40% to their estimates when planning."
  • Management task: Cannot be captured in a database — developed through experience, mentoring, and organizational culture

Flow: Data → (processing, context) → Information → (experience, judgment) → Knowledge → (reflection, application) → Wisdom

The SKMS is the conceptual framework for managing all knowledge in IT service management. It is NOT a single tool or database — it is a federated system of systems.

Key components within the SKMS:

  • CMDB (Configuration Management Database): Information about CIs, their attributes, and relationships (detailed in Topic 11)
  • Known Error Database (KEDB): Records of known errors and their workarounds/permanent fixes, maintained by problem management
  • Definitive Media Library (DML): Authorized versions of all software and documentation
  • Service Catalog: Published list of services available to users
  • Incident/Problem/Change Records: Historical data from operational processes
  • Supplier/Contract Database (SCMIS): Supplier information and contract details
  • Service Portfolio: Complete list of all services (pipeline, catalog, retired)

Knowledge Management Lifecycle: Capture, Store, Share, Use

1. Capture

  • From incidents: When a new issue is resolved, document the symptoms, diagnosis process, and solution
  • From problem management: Known errors and their workarounds/root causes
  • From changes: Post-implementation reviews, lessons learned
  • From people: Regular knowledge harvesting sessions to convert tacit knowledge to explicit
  • From projects: Post-project reviews, design documentation, operational handover guides
  • Challenge: Capturing tacit knowledge (the expertise in people's heads) is the hardest part. Use interviews, shadowing, pair working, and structured templates.

2. Store

  • Knowledge base articles: Searchable, categorized, tagged, version-controlled
  • Runbooks and procedures: Step-by-step operational guides
  • FAQs: Common questions with validated answers
  • Architecture documents: System designs, data flows, integration maps
  • Format standards: Consistent templates, metadata, review dates, ownership
  • Challenge: Knowledge rot — stored knowledge becomes outdated. Implement review cycles (every 3-6 months minimum).

3. Share

  • Self-service portals: Users can search and find solutions without contacting the service desk
  • Knowledge-centered service (KCS): Service desk agents search the knowledge base before creating new articles; every resolved incident potentially becomes a knowledge article
  • Internal wikis and collaboration platforms: Teams share knowledge across organizational boundaries
  • Training and mentoring: Structured programs to transfer knowledge between experienced and new staff
  • Communities of practice: Groups of people with shared expertise who learn from each other
  • Challenge: Cultural resistance ("knowledge is power" hoarding mentality). Must build a culture where sharing is valued and rewarded.

4. Use

  • Incident resolution: Service desk searches knowledge base for known solutions before escalating
  • Self-service deflection: Users solve their own issues using knowledge articles, reducing ticket volume
  • Decision support: Managers use accumulated knowledge to make informed decisions
  • Onboarding: New staff use knowledge base to ramp up quickly
  • Continual improvement: Knowledge analysis identifies recurring issues, process gaps, and improvement opportunities
  • Challenge: If knowledge exists but no one uses it, the entire effort is wasted. Measure knowledge article usage and integrate knowledge search into workflows.

Relationship to Incident Management

Knowledge management and incident management are deeply interconnected:

  • During incident resolution: Service desk agents search the knowledge base for known solutions. A good knowledge base dramatically reduces mean time to resolution (MTTR) and increases first-contact resolution (FCR).
  • After incident resolution: Novel solutions are captured as new knowledge articles. The incident record links to the knowledge article for future reference.
  • Known Error Database (KEDB): Problem management creates known error records (KERs) documenting errors with known root causes and documented workarounds. The service desk accesses these during incident handling to quickly identify whether an incident matches a known error.
  • Pattern detection: Analysis of incident records reveals patterns (recurring issues, common user errors, infrastructure weaknesses) that feed into problem management and continual improvement.

Self-Service Knowledge

Self-service knowledge enables users to resolve issues without contacting the service desk:

  • User-facing knowledge base: Curated articles written in plain language (not technical jargon), covering common issues, how-to guides, FAQs
  • Searchable portal: Integrated into the self-service portal alongside the service catalog
  • AI-powered suggestions: Modern implementations use AI to suggest relevant articles based on the user's search query or the content of their incident description
  • Knowledge article lifecycle: Draft → Review → Published → Archive. Only reviewed, approved articles are visible to users.
  • Metrics:
  • Self-service resolution rate (what percentage of users find answers without creating a ticket?)
  • Knowledge article usage (which articles are most/least viewed?)
  • Article effectiveness (did the article actually solve the problem?)
  • Search success rate (did the user find what they were looking for?)

Knowledge-Centered Service (KCS)

KCS (formerly Knowledge-Centered Support) is a methodology that integrates knowledge management into the daily work of the service desk, rather than treating it as a separate activity:

Four KCS practices: 1. Capture: Create or update knowledge articles as part of the problem-solving process (not after) 2. Structure: Use templates and consistent formatting for all knowledge articles 3. Reuse: Search for existing knowledge before creating new content; link incidents to knowledge articles 4. Improve: Continuously refine articles based on feedback, new information, and usage patterns

KCS principle: "Every interaction is an opportunity to capture knowledge." The marginal cost of capturing knowledge during incident resolution is near zero compared to creating it later from memory.

Key Metrics for Knowledge Management

  • Knowledge article count: Total articles, new articles per period, articles retired
  • Article usage rate: How often articles are viewed and by whom
  • Reuse rate: Percentage of incidents resolved using existing knowledge articles
  • Self-service deflection rate: Percentage of potential tickets avoided through self-service knowledge
  • First-contact resolution (FCR) rate: Higher FCR correlates with better knowledge availability
  • Knowledge contribution rate: How many staff are actively creating/updating knowledge articles
  • Article accuracy rate: Percentage of articles reported as inaccurate or outdated
  • Time to publish: How long from knowledge capture to article availability
  • Search success rate: Percentage of searches that return relevant results

Topic 11: Service Configuration Management and CMDB

Classification and Purpose

Category: Service Management Practice (one of the 17 service management practices in ITIL 4)

Purpose: To ensure that accurate and reliable information about the configuration of services and the Configuration Items (CIs) that support them is available when and where it is needed. This includes information about how CIs are configured and the relationships between them.

Key insight: Service configuration management is about understanding the current state of your IT environment — what you have, how it's connected, and what depends on what. Without this, incident management is guesswork, change management is risky, and problem management can't identify root causes.

What is the CMDB?

Configuration Management Database (CMDB): A database or set of databases used to store configuration records throughout their lifecycle. The CMDB records the attributes of each CI and the relationships between CIs.

CMDB vs. CMS: The Configuration Management System (CMS) is broader — it is a set of tools, data, and information used to support service configuration management. A CMS may contain one or more CMDBs plus other data sources (discovery tools, asset management systems, monitoring tools). The CMS provides the management layer; the CMDB is the data store.

CMDB vs. Asset Management: Often confused, these are different: - CMDB focuses on configuration (how things are set up, how they relate to each other, what services they support) - Asset management focuses on ownership (who owns it, what did it cost, when does the warranty expire, where is it physically located) - In practice, they often overlap and share data, but their purposes are different

Configuration Items (CIs)

What is a CI?

A Configuration Item is any component that needs to be managed in order to deliver an IT service. CIs are characterized by their attributes (recorded in the CI's configuration record) and their relationships to other CIs.

What Counts as a CI?

The scope of what is tracked as a CI is a critical design decision. Track too little and the CMDB is useless; track too much and it becomes unmaintainable.

Typical CI types:

Category Examples
Hardware Servers (physical and virtual), network switches, routers, firewalls, load balancers, storage arrays, workstations, laptops, printers, UPS systems
Software Operating systems, middleware, databases, applications (commercial and custom), firmware
Services IT services (as defined in the service catalog), business services that depend on IT services
Documents SLAs, OLAs, UCs, policies, procedures, runbooks, architecture documents, disaster recovery plans
Network VLANs, subnets, IP addresses, DNS records, certificates, firewall rules
Cloud Resources VMs, containers, cloud storage buckets, serverless functions, managed database instances
People/Teams Organizational units, support teams, key contacts (sometimes modeled as CIs, sometimes linked differently)
Facilities Data centers, server rooms, wiring closets, building infrastructure
Logical Business processes, service portfolios, projects

CI Attributes

Each CI record contains attributes that describe it. Common attributes include:

  • CI identifier: Unique ID (e.g., SRV-PRD-WEB-001)
  • CI name: Human-readable name
  • CI type/category: Classification (server, application, network device, etc.)
  • Description: What the CI is and what it does
  • Owner: Person or team responsible for the CI
  • Status: Current lifecycle status (planned, ordered, received, in test, live/production, retired, disposed)
  • Location: Physical or logical location
  • Version: Current version or firmware level
  • Vendor/Supplier: Who made or provides it
  • Serial/License number: Unique manufacturer or license identifier
  • Installation date: When it was put into service
  • Last modified date: When the CI record was last updated
  • Support group: Team responsible for supporting the CI
  • Service(s) supported: Which IT services depend on this CI
  • Criticality/Business impact: How important is this CI to service delivery

CI Relationships and Dependency Mapping

Relationships between CIs are as important as the CIs themselves. Dependency mapping shows how CIs connect and depend on each other.

Relationship Types

  • Runs on / Hosted on: Application X runs on Server Y
  • Connected to: Server A is connected to Switch B
  • Depends on: Service Z depends on Database D
  • Is part of: Hard drive H is part of Server S
  • Uses: Application P uses Database Q
  • Managed by: CI is managed by Team T
  • Backed up by: Server A is backed up by Backup System B
  • Clustered with: Server A is clustered with Server B for high availability

Why Dependency Mapping Matters

  • Impact analysis for changes: Before making a change to Server X, the CMDB shows all services and CIs that depend on Server X. This enables accurate impact assessment: "If we take this server down for maintenance, these 5 services and 3,000 users will be affected."
  • Root cause analysis for incidents: When a service is degraded, the CMDB shows all underlying CIs. Correlating CI alerts with the dependency map pinpoints the root cause quickly.
  • Business impact assessment: When a CI fails, the CMDB shows which business services are affected and how critical they are. This enables proper incident prioritization.
  • Disaster recovery planning: Understanding dependencies helps design recovery sequences — which CIs must be recovered first to restore the most critical services.

How CMDB Supports Other ITSM Practices

Incident Management

  • Impact assessment: Immediately see what services and users are affected by a CI failure
  • Root cause identification: Trace the dependency chain from symptomatic CI back to the root cause CI
  • Communication: Know who to notify based on affected services
  • Workaround identification: See alternative CIs or paths that could provide temporary service restoration

Change Enablement

  • Impact assessment: Before approving a change, assess which CIs and services will be affected
  • Risk evaluation: Understand the blast radius if the change goes wrong
  • Planning: Identify all teams that need to be involved based on CI ownership
  • Post-implementation verification: Confirm that CI records are updated after the change

Problem Management

  • Trend analysis: Identify CIs with recurring incidents (hardware failing repeatedly, software with frequent crashes)
  • Root cause investigation: Use relationship maps to trace problems to their source
  • Known error documentation: Link known errors to affected CIs
  • Proactive problem identification: Analyze CMDB data to find vulnerable configurations before they cause incidents

Service Level Management

  • Service-to-CI mapping: Understand which CIs underpin each SLA
  • Capacity planning: Track CI utilization to predict capacity needs
  • Availability reporting: Calculate service availability based on CI uptime data

Why CMDB Accuracy Matters

An inaccurate CMDB is worse than no CMDB at all — it creates false confidence and leads to incorrect decisions.

Consequences of inaccuracy: - Changes approved based on wrong dependency data cause unexpected outages - Incidents take longer to resolve because the recorded configuration doesn't match reality - Security vulnerabilities go undetected because the CMDB doesn't reflect what's actually deployed - Compliance audits fail because records don't match the actual environment - Cost calculations are wrong because the CMDB doesn't reflect what's actually in use

How to maintain accuracy: 1. Automated discovery: Use network discovery tools to automatically detect and update CI information. Never rely solely on manual updates. 2. Reconciliation: Regularly compare CMDB data with actual environment data (discovery scans, asset inventories, monitoring data). Identify and resolve discrepancies. 3. Change integration: Every change that affects a CI must update the CMDB as part of the change process. The change is not complete until the CMDB is updated. 4. Verification audits: Periodic audits comparing CMDB records to the actual state of the infrastructure. Sample-based audits are practical; 100% audits are usually not feasible. 5. Ownership and accountability: Every CI must have a designated owner who is responsible for keeping the CI record current. 6. Scope management: Only track CIs that you will actively manage and keep current. An overly ambitious scope leads to stale data.

Common CMDB Pitfalls

  1. "Boil the ocean" approach: Trying to track everything from day one. Start with critical services and their supporting CIs, then expand incrementally.
  2. Manual-only population: Relying on people to manually create and update CI records. Use automated discovery and integrate CMDB updates into workflows.
  3. No defined CI scope: Not deciding in advance what counts as a CI and what doesn't. This leads to inconsistency and scope creep.
  4. No maintenance process: Building the CMDB once and not maintaining it. CMDB data degrades rapidly without ongoing verification and updates.
  5. Treating CMDB as an asset database: The CMDB tracks configuration and relationships, not just inventory. Without relationship data, the CMDB is just an expensive spreadsheet.
  6. Ignoring relationship data: Tracking CIs but not their relationships. The relationships (dependencies) are where the real value lies.
  7. Poor data quality standards: No validation rules, no mandatory fields, no naming conventions. Garbage in = garbage out.
  8. Lack of organizational buy-in: CMDB requires all teams to participate in maintaining data. Without leadership mandate and cultural adoption, data quality erodes.
  9. Tool-first approach: Buying a CMDB tool before defining processes, scope, and governance. The tool doesn't solve organizational problems.
  10. No integration with other ITSM processes: A CMDB that isn't integrated with incident, change, and problem management delivers no value. Integration is not optional.

Federation vs. Centralization

Modern best practice favors a federated CMDB approach:

  • Federated: Multiple authoritative sources contribute CI data to a unified view. The CMDB doesn't try to be the single source of truth for all data — it integrates with discovery tools, monitoring systems, asset databases, and cloud management platforms. Each system is authoritative for the data it manages best.
  • Centralized: One massive database tries to contain all CI data. Harder to maintain, harder to integrate, creates a bottleneck.

Practical implementation: Use a CMDB tool that can auto-discover and sync with your environment (network discovery, VMware/cloud APIs, Active Directory, etc.), with manual enrichment for relationship data and business context that can't be auto-discovered.

Topic 12: Service Desk

Classification and Purpose

Category: Service Management Practice (one of the 17 service management practices in ITIL 4)

Purpose: To capture demand for incident resolution and service requests. It is also the entry point and single point of contact for the service provider with all of its users. The service desk practice is fundamental to the value chain activity "Engage."

ITIL 4 evolution: In ITIL v3, the service desk was classified as a "function" (an organizational unit). In ITIL 4, it is elevated to a "practice" — recognizing that it involves people, processes, technology, and partners, not just a team. ITIL 4 also emphasizes the service desk's role in business value creation, not just technical support.

Service Desk as the Single Point of Contact (SPOC)

The SPOC concept means that the service desk is the ONE entry point for users to engage with IT service management. All service interactions flow through the service desk:

  • Incident reporting: "Something is broken" — users report issues
  • Service requests: "I need something" — users request services, access, information
  • Queries and information: "How do I...?" — users seek guidance
  • Feedback and complaints: "This isn't working well" — users provide input for improvement

Why SPOC matters: - Users have ONE number to call, ONE portal to use — no confusion about where to go - All interactions are logged, tracked, and measurable - Prevents issues from being reported directly to technical teams (bypassing tracking) - Enables consistent service experience regardless of which agent handles the request - Provides a complete picture of IT demand and user needs

Service Desk vs. Help Desk

Aspect Help Desk Service Desk
Focus Break-fix (something is broken, fix it) End-to-end service management
Scope Technical issues only Incidents, requests, queries, complaints, feedback
Approach Reactive (respond when contacted) Proactive + reactive (monitor, anticipate, prevent)
Business alignment Limited — technology-focused High — understands business impact and priority
Value proposition Restore functionality Enable business productivity and user satisfaction
Processes Basic ticketing and escalation Integrated with all ITSM practices
Maturity Basic (often the starting point) Advanced (evolved from help desk)

ITIL 4 perspective: The service desk is not just a "help desk with a fancier name." It is a strategic function that: - Understands business priority and impact - Manages the user relationship - Provides a communication channel between IT and the business - Contributes to service improvement through feedback and pattern recognition - Enables self-service and knowledge sharing

Service Desk Channels

Modern service desks support multiple channels — users should be able to contact the service desk through their preferred method:

Channel Description Best For
Phone/Voice Traditional call center model Urgent/critical issues, complex problems needing real-time dialogue, users who prefer human interaction
Email Users email the service desk Non-urgent issues, detailed descriptions, attachment of screenshots/logs
Self-Service Portal Web-based portal where users log tickets, browse service catalog, search knowledge base Service requests, password resets, standard changes, knowledge search, tracking existing tickets
Chat/Messaging Real-time text chat (live agent or chatbot) Quick questions, simple issues, users who prefer text, initial triage before escalation
Walk-in Physical service desk location Hardware issues requiring physical access, in-person assistance, new employee setup
Social Media Monitoring and responding via Twitter, Slack, Teams channels Internal communications in organizations using these platforms, broad announcements
Mobile App Smartphone application for ticket submission and tracking On-the-go reporting, field workers, push notifications for updates
Automated/Event-Driven Monitoring tools automatically create incidents without human initiation Infrastructure alerts, threshold breaches, automated failover events

Omnichannel principle: Regardless of how the user contacts the service desk, the experience should be consistent, and the interaction should be tracked in the same system. A user who starts a chat should be able to follow up by email without repeating their issue.

Tiered Support Model (L1, L2, L3)

The tiered support model organizes technical support into escalation levels:

Level 0 (L0) — Self-Service

  • Who: The user themselves
  • What: Password resets via self-service portal, knowledge base searches, FAQ articles, automated provisioning
  • Goal: Resolve issues without human intervention
  • Metric: Self-service adoption rate, deflection rate

Level 1 (L1) — Service Desk / First Line

  • Who: Service desk agents (generalists, first point of contact)
  • What: Initial triage, categorization, prioritization, basic troubleshooting, known solution application, service request fulfillment
  • Skills: Broad but shallow technical knowledge, strong communication and customer service skills, knowledge base proficiency
  • Goal: Resolve as many issues as possible at first contact (first-contact resolution)
  • Escalation trigger: Issue exceeds L1 knowledge or capability, requires specialized skills, or exceeds time threshold

Level 2 (L2) — Technical Support / Second Line

  • Who: Technical specialists (network engineers, system administrators, application support teams)
  • What: In-depth technical investigation, complex troubleshooting, configuration changes, workaround development
  • Skills: Deep expertise in specific technology domains
  • Goal: Resolve issues that L1 cannot, within SLA timeframes
  • Escalation trigger: Issue requires vendor support, code changes, architectural changes, or exceeds L2 capability

Level 3 (L3) — Expert Support / Third Line

  • Who: Subject matter experts, developers, architects, vendor support
  • What: Root cause analysis, code fixes, design changes, product bugs, complex integrations
  • Skills: Deepest technical expertise, often the people who built or designed the system
  • Goal: Permanent resolution of complex or novel issues
  • Note: L3 often includes external vendor support (e.g., BMC, Microsoft, Cisco support)

Level 4 (L4) — External/Vendor Support (sometimes defined)

  • Who: External product vendors and manufacturers
  • What: Product bugs, firmware issues, proprietary system problems
  • Engagement: Managed through supplier management and underpinning contracts

Shift-Left Strategy: The goal is to move resolution closer to the user — push knowledge and tools from L3 → L2 → L1 → L0 (self-service). Every issue resolved at a lower tier costs less and resolves faster.

Service Desk Types

Type Description Best For Challenges
Local Service Desk Physically located at the user site. Direct face-to-face support. Small organizations, single-location operations, environments requiring physical presence Expensive to staff, limited hours, doesn't scale
Centralized Service Desk Single service desk serving the entire organization from one location Medium-to-large organizations seeking efficiency and standardization May lack local context, timezone challenges for global organizations
Virtual Service Desk Agents work from various locations (including remotely) but appear as a single team to users Distributed/global organizations, remote workforce support Requires robust tools and processes, harder to manage team culture

Service Desk Metrics and KPIs

Volume Metrics

  • Ticket volume: Total tickets per period (day/week/month), by category, by priority
  • Ticket volume trends: Is volume increasing or decreasing? What's driving changes?
  • Tickets by channel: How are users contacting the service desk? Which channels are growing?
  • Backlog: Number of open tickets at any point in time

Speed Metrics

  • First response time: How quickly does the service desk acknowledge a user's contact?
  • Average handle time (AHT): Average time spent on each interaction
  • Mean time to resolve (MTTR): Average time from ticket creation to resolution
  • SLA compliance rate: Percentage of tickets resolved within SLA targets

Quality Metrics

  • First-contact resolution (FCR): Percentage of tickets resolved during the first interaction without escalation. Industry benchmark: 70-75% is good.
  • Reopened ticket rate: Percentage of tickets that are reopened after closure (indicates incomplete resolution). Target: below 5%.
  • Escalation rate: Percentage of tickets escalated to L2 or L3. Lower is generally better (indicates L1 competence).
  • Customer satisfaction (CSAT): User satisfaction score from post-interaction surveys. Typically measured on a 1-5 scale.
  • Net Promoter Score (NPS): "How likely are you to recommend our service desk?" Broader satisfaction indicator.

Efficiency Metrics

  • Cost per ticket: Total service desk cost divided by ticket volume. Used for benchmarking and efficiency tracking.
  • Agent utilization: Percentage of agent time spent on productive activities (handling tickets, creating knowledge). Target: 60-75%.
  • Self-service adoption rate: Percentage of users using self-service channels vs. contacting agents.
  • Knowledge base usage: How often are knowledge articles used in ticket resolution?

Strategic Metrics

  • Shift-left rate: Are more issues being resolved at lower tiers over time?
  • Problem ticket ratio: What percentage of incidents lead to problem investigations? Indicates proactive problem management maturity.
  • User experience score: Broader measure of how users perceive IT service quality (may include XLA-based metrics).

Service Empathy in ITIL 4

ITIL 4 emphasizes service empathy — understanding the user's situation and emotional state, not just the technical issue:

  • A user reporting a laptop failure before a critical presentation has different emotional needs than one reporting a printer issue
  • Service desk agents should be trained in emotional intelligence, active listening, and communication skills — not just technical troubleshooting
  • Service empathy drives CSAT scores and user perception of IT more than technical resolution speed alone
  • The service desk is often the "face of IT" — every interaction shapes the organization's perception of IT service quality

Topic 13: Continual Improvement

Classification and Purpose

Category: Both a component of the Service Value System (SVS) AND a General Management Practice. Continual improvement operates at two levels: 1. SVS component: An overarching principle that applies across the entire SVS — every activity, every practice, every service should be subject to continual improvement 2. Practice: A specific set of organizational resources for implementing improvement initiatives

Purpose: To align the organization's practices and services with changing business needs through the ongoing identification and improvement of services, service components, practices, or any element involved in the efficient and effective management of products and services.

ITIL 4 name change: Renamed from "Continual Service Improvement" (CSI) in ITIL v3 to simply "Continual Improvement" in ITIL 4 — reflecting that improvement applies to everything (products, services, practices, processes, technology, people), not just services.

The Continual Improvement Model

ITIL 4 defines a structured model with 6 steps (evolved from the 7-step improvement process in ITIL v3):

Step 1: What is the vision?

  • Understand the organization's high-level direction, mission, and objectives
  • Every improvement must align with the organizational vision and strategy
  • Without clear vision, improvements may optimize the wrong things
  • Key question: "What are we trying to achieve overall?"
  • Output: Clear improvement direction aligned with business strategy

Step 2: Where are we now?

  • Assess the current state honestly and objectively
  • Use data, measurements, and direct observation — not assumptions
  • Establish baselines for metrics you intend to improve
  • Understand current strengths (to leverage) and weaknesses (to address)
  • Key question: "What is our starting point? What do the measurements tell us?"
  • Tools: Maturity assessments, SWOT analysis, current-state process mapping, benchmarking against industry peers
  • Output: Documented current state with baseline measurements

Step 3: Where do we want to be?

  • Define specific, measurable improvement targets
  • Prioritize what matters most — you can't improve everything at once
  • Use gap analysis: current state vs. desired state
  • Set realistic targets with defined timeframes
  • Key question: "What does 'good' look like? What specific metrics will improve by how much?"
  • Output: Measurable targets, prioritized improvement objectives, success criteria

Step 4: How do we get there?

  • Design the improvement plan — specific actions, resources, timelines, responsibilities
  • Consider multiple approaches and select the most effective one
  • Plan for iterations — test on a small scale before rolling out broadly
  • Identify risks and mitigation strategies
  • Key question: "What specific actions will close the gap between current and desired state?"
  • Output: Detailed improvement plan with milestones, resources, and risk assessment

Step 5: Take action

  • Execute the improvement plan
  • This step was added in ITIL 4 (not present in the v3 7-step process) to emphasize that execution matters — many organizations plan improvements but fail to implement them
  • Use iterative/Agile approaches: implement in small increments, gather feedback, adjust
  • Communicate progress to stakeholders
  • Key question: "Are we actually doing what we planned? Are we seeing early results?"
  • Output: Implemented improvements, progress data, lessons learned during execution

Step 6: Did we get there?

  • Evaluate the results of the improvement against the targets defined in Step 3
  • Measure whether the improvement achieved its objectives
  • If targets were met: standardize the improvement and embed it in normal operations
  • If targets were not met: analyze why and determine next steps (try again, adjust approach, or abandon)
  • Feed lessons learned back into the next improvement cycle
  • Key question: "Did we achieve what we set out to achieve? What did we learn?"
  • Output: Evaluation results, lessons learned, updated baselines, input to next improvement cycle

The model is iterative: Step 6 feeds back to Step 1 or Step 2, creating a continuous cycle. The model can also be entered at any step — you don't always need to start from Step 1.

Relationship to the PDCA (Deming) Cycle

The continual improvement model maps to the Plan-Do-Check-Act cycle:

PDCA Improvement Model Steps
Plan Steps 1-4 (Vision, Where are we now, Where do we want to be, How do we get there)
Do Step 5 (Take action)
Check Step 6 (Did we get there?)

The Continual Improvement Register (CIR)

The CIR (formerly CSI Register in ITIL v3) is the database or structured document that tracks all improvement ideas, initiatives, and their status. It is the single, prioritized backlog for improvement.

What the CIR contains for each improvement idea: - Identifier: Unique ID for tracking - Description: What the improvement is about - Source: Where the idea came from (incident analysis, user feedback, management review, audit, benchmark) - Priority: How important is this improvement relative to others - Justification/Business case: Why this improvement matters, expected benefits - Status: New → Assessed → Approved → In Progress → Completed → Closed - Owner: Who is responsible for driving this improvement - Target metrics: What specific measurement will demonstrate success - Timeline: Expected start, milestones, completion date - Resources required: People, budget, technology needed - Outcome: What actually happened (documented at completion)

CIR management practices: - Regular review meetings (weekly or biweekly) to assess, prioritize, and track improvements - Visible to all stakeholders — transparency about what's being improved and why - Linked to governance — approved improvements receive resources and management attention - Not just a wish list — each item must have an owner, a priority, and a realistic plan - Limited work in progress — don't start more improvements than you can effectively manage

The 7-Step Improvement Process (ITIL v3 — Still Relevant Context)

The v3 process had 7 specific steps focused on measurement-driven improvement:

  1. Identify the strategy for improvement: What should we measure?
  2. Define what you will measure: What can we measure? (Gap between "should" and "can" is important to recognize)
  3. Gather the data: Collect raw data through monitoring, measuring, and data collection
  4. Process the data: Convert raw data into the required format (aggregate, normalize, correlate)
  5. Analyze the information and data: Transform data into knowledge — trends, patterns, root causes, deviations
  6. Present and use the information: Create reports and presentations for target audiences; use information to drive decisions
  7. Implement improvement: Take corrective actions based on the knowledge gained

ITIL 4 simplification: The ITIL 4 model condenses these 7 steps into 6, with the measurement/analysis activities distributed across the steps rather than being separate steps themselves. The key addition is "Take Action" (Step 5), which v3 lacked as a distinct step.

Maturity Models

Maturity models provide a structured way to assess the current capability of practices and identify improvement targets:

Generic ITIL Maturity Levels

Level Name Description
0 None No process exists. Work is ad hoc and chaotic.
1 Initial Process is recognized but unstructured. Success depends on individual effort, not repeatable process.
2 Repeatable Basic processes are established. Similar tasks are handled consistently. Some documentation exists.
3 Defined Processes are documented, standardized, and integrated into standard practices. Training exists. Consistent outcomes.
4 Managed Processes are measured and controlled. Quantitative metrics are collected and used for management decisions. Performance is predictable.
5 Optimizing Focus is on continual improvement of process performance. Innovation and optimization are embedded. Processes are continuously refined based on data.

Practical use of maturity models: - Assess current maturity of each practice (where are we now? — Step 2) - Set target maturity levels (where do we want to be? — Step 3) - Identify specific gaps between current and target maturity - Prioritize improvement efforts — not every practice needs to be at Level 5. Focus on practices that matter most to the business. - Track maturity progression over time as evidence of improvement

Important caveat: Higher maturity is not always better. A Level 3 maturity for a non-critical practice may be perfectly appropriate. The target maturity should be determined by business need, not by a desire to score 5 everywhere.

How to Measure and Improve IT Services Over Time

Types of Metrics

  1. Technology metrics: Component-level measurements (CPU utilization, network latency, disk I/O). Important for operational management but not directly meaningful to business.

  2. Process metrics: Measurements of practice/process performance (change success rate, incident MTTR, problem resolution rate). Important for service management improvement.

  3. Service metrics: End-to-end measurements of service performance as experienced by users (service availability, service response time, user satisfaction). Most meaningful to business stakeholders.

Metric hierarchy: Technology metrics feed into process metrics, which feed into service metrics. A service availability issue might be caused by a process failure (e.g., poor change management) which in turn relates to a technology metric (e.g., server overload).

Critical Success Factors (CSFs) and KPIs

  • CSF: A high-level condition that must be met for improvement to succeed. Example: "IT leadership actively supports and sponsors improvement initiatives."
  • KPI: A specific, measurable indicator that demonstrates whether a CSF is being met. Example: "Percentage of approved improvement initiatives that receive funding within 30 days: target >80%."

Each improvement initiative should have: - 2-3 CSFs (conditions for success) - 2-5 KPIs per CSF (measurable indicators) - Defined targets and thresholds for each KPI - Regular reporting cadence

Establishing Baselines

  • A baseline is the starting measurement against which improvement is measured
  • Before starting any improvement, document the current state quantitatively
  • Without baselines, you cannot demonstrate improvement — "we improved response time" means nothing without "from X to Y"
  • Baselines should be stable (taken over a representative period, not a single day)
  • Re-baseline periodically as the organization evolves

Continual Improvement and Organizational Culture

Improvement is fundamentally a cultural practice, not just a process:

  • Leadership commitment: Leaders must visibly sponsor and participate in improvement activities. Without this, improvement is deprioritized against "real work."
  • Safe-to-fail culture: People must feel safe to identify problems, suggest improvements, and experiment without fear of blame.
  • Data-driven decisions: Base improvement priorities on evidence and data, not opinions or politics.
  • Recognition and reward: Acknowledge and reward people who contribute to improvement — both successful improvements and valuable lessons from failures.
  • Embedded in daily work: Improvement should not be a separate activity or a quarterly exercise. It should be part of how people work every day.
  • Learning from failure: Not every improvement will succeed. The value is in learning what doesn't work and why, not just in celebrating successes.

Key Metrics for Continual Improvement

  • Number of improvement initiatives: How many improvements are in the CIR (pipeline, in progress, completed)?
  • Improvement completion rate: What percentage of approved improvements are completed successfully?
  • Time from idea to implementation: How long does it take from identifying an improvement to realizing its benefits?
  • Improvement ROI: What is the measurable benefit of completed improvements vs. their cost?
  • Employee engagement in improvement: How many staff are contributing improvement ideas?
  • Maturity progression: Are practice maturity scores improving over time?
  • Recurring issue reduction: Are the same incidents/problems recurring less frequently over time?
